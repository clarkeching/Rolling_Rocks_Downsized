## Fowl Mood

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve chickens out of confronting Hal. Instead, he tells Gregor to cancel the compulsory overtime and cancelled vacations. Gregor asks: "What would you have done differently?" Steve has no answer.*

*Late August.*

Steve was in a fowl mood when he made it back to his desk. Yes, *fowl*, not foul. Somewhere between the second and fourth floors, the rational part of his brain clicked in. He turned into a big chicken and pushed the button for the sixth floor instead of the penthouse. He'd spent almost twenty years climbing the Wyxcomb ladder, and he wasn't about to throw that all away impulsively.

Instead of giving Hal an excuse to dump him, he summoned Gregor to his office and laid down the law: no compulsory overtime, no cancelled vacations.

Gregor's response was a question.

"What would you have done differently?"

"Pardon?"

"If you were in my shoes. What would you have done differently?"

Steve took a deep breath and did the only thing he could. He apologised. Gregor was right. He had done exactly what Steve would have done in his shoes. And worse, Steve was doing exactly what his predecessors would have done in his situation. He told Gregor that.

Gregor shrugged. He was old enough, ugly enough and grumpy enough to be thick-skinned enough to know that sometimes taking the blame was just part of his job.

Steve shook his head. "But it shouldn't be. We should be better at this."

They left it at that. Steve sat at his desk and gave up scrolling through his emails when he got to one from Hal's personal assistant asking *him* to look at her printer because it was jamming a lot. He shook his head, swore briefly under his breath, and called the help desk manager.

## Start Looking for a New Job

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve calls Norbert, who delivers brutal news: he can't protect Steve if FPP fails. Norbert's advice: beg Craig Lally for help—"Maybe he can see things the rest of us can't." And start looking for a new job.*

Steve tried to call Eleanor, but she was mid-flight to Sweden with Hal.

So he called Norbert.

Norbert answered immediately and without saying hello. "Can you deliver FPP on December 1st?"

"Realistically?"

"Of course, realistically. We are not playing computer games. You do not have three lives to play with. I ask you again: will you deliver or not?"

Steve didn't know what to say.

"Yes or no. Quick."

"No."

"That's what I thought." The phone went silent for a long moment. "I don't know if I can help you. If FPP doesn't ship, then Hal is gone. It will be difficult for me to protect you under those circumstances."

He may as well have slapped Steve across the face. Norbert wasn't just his boss—he was his mentor and protector. After Fran died, it was Norbert who pulled every bureaucratic string and corporate lever necessary to transfer Steve from Glasgow to Watt's Bridge, nearer his home and his mother and the kids. Eleanor hadn't wanted him—she wanted someone with smaller ambitions—but Norbert had insisted. In Wyxcomb, he said, we looked after our own.

His words hurt. Norbert knew the job market in Watt's Bridge for people like Steve was limited. He knew about the kids, about his mother, about the fact that commuting to Edinburgh or Glasgow was out of the question and relocating was worse.

Steve let his breath out. "I need your help, Norbert."

Norbert said nothing.

Steve waited a few seconds, then asked, "What should I do?"

"You talked to your quality guy, Lally. Tell me how that went."

Steve chose his words carefully. "He couldn't help me with FPP. That was, and still is, my priority. He wanted to help me prevent my *next* FPP."

"I spoke to him. He said your conversation was brief, that you seemed reluctant to engage further but that you parted on good terms."

So Craig had kept his promise.

"When I pressed him, he said he was certain he could help you, but that you'd have to ask for help if you wanted it."

"Oh."

"My advice is for you to ask for his help. Beg if you have to, but get his help."

Steve bit down on his lip. "But why him?"

"Because frankly, I don't know anyone else who wouldn't do precisely what you've already done. I am clutching at straws here. Maybe he can see things the rest of us can't."

"But what if he can't help me?"

"Then you're no worse off." Norbert was silent for a moment. "Maybe you should start looking for a new job, Steve. Leave under your own terms. You have a few months before things come to a head."

Then he hung up.

Steve put the phone down and rested his head in his hands. The day had started well, but then—due to circumstances beyond his control and despite everything he'd given to Wyxcomb—he'd become expendable.

Five very long minutes passed. It felt like more. Eventually he looked up, forced a smile onto his face, and dialled Craig Lally. It was 3:56 p.m.

## Ten Minutes

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve swallows his pride and calls Craig, who reluctantly agrees to give him ten minutes. Craig's son is getting married this weekend and he's off the grid for three weeks. Norbert has a message: "If you walk out on me this time, call him. He'll fly here and take over your job."*

Craig answered, then paused. "I didn't expect to hear from you again."

He sounded more distracted than surprised.

Steve apologised for not calling back sooner, then asked if Craig could spare him thirty minutes.

"Thirty minutes? I don't have thirty minutes to spare."

"Fifteen, then?"

"I'm afraid I am very busy just now."

Steve steeled his jaw. "I'm desperate. I swear I wouldn't ask otherwise."

Another pause. "Yes, okay. I'll give you ten minutes, but no more."

Steve thanked him. He had no intention of sticking to ten minutes.

"We're experiencing a few little problems."

"Little problems? You said you're desperate."

"No, desperate is accurate."

He spent the first eight of his ten minutes explaining why he was so desperate, glossing over the details of the mutiny. Craig summed it up matter-of-factly: Hal expected FPP by December 1st, Steve couldn't predict how long the remaining features would take to build and test, therefore he couldn't keep his promise. Was that it?

"Yes."

"The cloud we drew. Has anything changed?"

"It's the same cloud, just much worse. The date's shrunk and the only way we can possibly deliver on time is to cut more features and risk a shoddier product."

"Let's use that pressure to our advantage. A noose around one's neck, they say, is very good at focusing one's thoughts."

Steve didn't laugh. It was *his* neck Craig was talking about.

Craig told Steve to hold. Steve heard muffled conversation in the background. A few minutes later, Craig called back.

"I've just spoken with Norbert. I've cleared my diary for the afternoon. I'll meet you downstairs, in the cafeteria, in twenty minutes. Bring your FPP folder with you."

Steve dropped the FPP folder on the cafeteria table with a *thunk*. The cafeteria was mostly empty—a few evening shift workers in comfortable, casual clothes were queuing at the serving area for their subsidised dinners.

Craig ignored the folder. "Norbert confirmed your job is at risk. He asked me to pass on a message." He placed his phone on the table. "If you walk out on me this time, call him. He said he'll fly here and take over your job himself."

Steve flinched. "He couldn't do my job."

Craig shrugged, message delivered, and moved on. "My son gets married this weekend. The wedding rehearsal is this evening. My family has flown in from around the world and I'm on vacation for three weeks. So your timing, from your point of view, sucks." He allowed himself a grin. "From my point of view, I'm on holiday for three weeks."

Steve could feel the noose tighten. Out of politeness, he said, "Congratulations. We should get started then."

"Excellent!" Craig boomed. He stood. "Leave your folder there. Follow me." They started walking towards the kitchen and he said, "There's just one rule I want you to follow."

"What's that?"

"Do not think about small batches. Okay?"

"Small what?"

"Batches. Don't think about them."

## The French Fry Revelation

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Cheryl reveals the secret of never running out of chips: cook small batches and replenish on demand. The cafeteria went from near-bankruptcy to a thirty percent increase in customers, a forty percent increase in revenue, and every job saved.*

Cheryl's eyes narrowed when she saw Steve. "You again?"

He deserved that. Last time he'd been in this kitchen, she'd offered to tell him the second half of her story—the half that tied everything together—and he'd glanced at his watch and left.

"I should have stayed for the French Fries," he said.

She studied his face. Something was different. Less polished. More worn. Whatever had been happening upstairs, it had taken something out of him.

"Last time, you heard about our Dog Food Problem," she said. "You remember what that taught us?"

"Test your own product. Build quality in as you go, not at the end."

She nodded, pleased he'd been paying attention. "Good. That was the first step. But it was only the first step." She led them through to the serving area, past the bain-maries where food sat under hot lamps. "Better food brought us more customers. And more customers brought us a whole new problem."

She pointed at the deep fryers. Three of them, side by side.

"Tell me. What would happen if we ever ran out of chips?"

"You'd all be strung up outside the castle gates."

She nodded. "Would that happen if we ran out of salad, or soup?"

"Not much. Maybe people would eat chips instead."

"Unspoken rule Number One around here: don't run out of chips. Rule Two: don't serve cold chips." She fixed him with a look. "Some things are sacred, Steve."

"Our French Fry Revelation happened when Craig asked me why we never ran out of chips and yet we rarely threw chips away from cooking too many." She glanced at Craig, who was absorbed in reading a safety notice pinned beside the fryers. "You know what I said to him?"

"No idea."

"It's obvious! Our serving staff keeps an eye on how many chips are out front, and when it gets too low, they shout and we cook more." She paused. "Craig calls it *replenishing on demand*. Has he asked you not to think about small batches yet?"

Steve looked at Craig, still reading his safety notice. "He has."

"He did the same to me. It was like he was an oyster farmer and he'd planted a bit of grit inside my shell, hoping I'd turn it into a pearl." She shook her head, remembering. "I spent days trying not to think about small batches. Of course, the opposite happened. I figured out how to cook our vegetables in small batches instead of big ones."

She walked him back past the fryers to the prep area. "We used to cook everything in huge pots. Big batch of peas, big batch of cabbage, big batch of carrots. All went on at half ten. Then we'd move it to the bain-maries and keep it warm until people came for lunch."

"Have you ever tasted two-hour-old cabbage?" she asked.

"My grandmother was an expert at one-hour-old cabbage."

Cheryl laughed—a real laugh, not a polite one—and slapped him on the arm. "Your grandmother would have fitted right in here. Two-hour-old is worse, trust me. The food wasn't too bad if you ate at noon, but by two o'clock it was horrid."

She explained the tricks they'd used to slow the deterioration. Loads more oil in a big pot of curry so it'd sit longer without burning. "Just remember that next time you're tempted by a cheap takeaway," she said.

Steve said he would.

"Nowadays, we prep everything before the rush—clean, peel, slice, dice—and cook the first small batch just before lunch. During service, the servers keep an eye on what's selling. They shout to the kitchen when we need more."

"What happens if you're really busy? Don't you risk not keeping up?"

"Could happen. Doesn't. Can you figure out why?"

Steve put his hand to his chin. "You cook in larger batches when demand is high?"

"Bingo!" she said. "Give the man a cigar."

Strange. That was the second time someone had offered him a cigar that day.

There was another problem too, she told him. Some days they'd cook too much soup and not enough lasagna. They'd guess at the start of each week what to produce, and they almost always got it wrong. Craig called it overproduction and underproduction—both bad, but losing sales because you didn't have what customers wanted was usually worse.

"So we started cooking soup in smaller batches," Cheryl said. "If a flavour wasn't popular, we'd put on a different one. If it was selling fast, we'd cook a bigger batch of the same."

She was warming to her story now. She described how her team had conducted what she called "a little clandestine commercial espionage"—taking turns visiting the good lunch shops on the High Street, spying on how they worked. They'd used their early profits to buy two small ovens like the ones Subway used and started baking fresh rolls throughout the lunch break. They'd replaced two bain-maries with a sandwich bar, since they no longer needed the storage space.

Her face beamed. "Not bad, eh?"

Craig said, "Not bad at all."

Steve could see she was proud, and rightly so. By the end of the first month, they were serving thirty percent more customers. Revenue was up forty percent. Every job saved.

It all made perfect sense. To them.

But Steve had no idea how any of it applied to his situation. He didn't know what small batches meant in a software context. And he suspected Craig didn't either. Craig might hope the principles from Cheryl's kitchen applied to software development, but Steve was certainly a guinea pig. For the next few hours, though, he decided he would cross his fingers and hope Craig was right. He had nothing left to lose.

Craig thanked Cheryl and they made their way back to their seats out front. They both checked their phones. Steve had a dozen low-priority emails and a voicemail from Eleanor which more or less told him she'd spoken to Norbert, and that he was to work with Craig or pack his bags. Nice. He powered the phone down in case she called again.

"There's one more thing Cheryl's team did that she forgot to mention," Craig said. "They thinned their menu. Removed all but their most popular dishes. It gave them wiggle room to learn how to work the new way, and they gradually added dishes back over time."

It would be some time before Steve realised how important that step—thinning the menu—would be.

## Requirements Are Really Forecasts

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Craig challenges Steve's assumptions: "requirements" are really just forecasts. Steve resists, fights, sulks—but Craig is right and Steve knows it. Craig leaves for his son's wedding rehearsal and gives Steve homework: think about small batches, and call Catherine.*

Craig picked up the 312-page FPP requirements folder and weighed it in his hands, flipping through the pages before handing it back.

"Since you're desperate, we'll skip the formal process and figure out whether Cheryl's lessons apply to your work." He tapped the folder. "These things you call *requirements*—I don't think that word means what you think it means."

Steve said nothing.

"If they were genuinely *required*"—Craig made little quote marks with his fingers—"then that implies they were mandatory. And if they were mandatory, how could your customers descope them? How could they possibly be required one minute, then not required the next?"

Steve's face felt hot—from embarrassment or anger, he wasn't sure. As much as his instinct was to fight, Craig was making sense.

"When your team wrote this document, it was a forecast. Nothing more than a prediction."

Steve's voice wanted to rise into a defensive whine, but he fought it. "We put a lot of effort into getting that right."

"But it wasn't right, was it? How many times have you descoped?"

"Twice."

"So your end date was a forecast too?"

"An estimate."

"Same thing."

Steve banged his fist in the palm of his hand. "You're playing word games with me!"

"It's annoying, isn't it? But the words matter. Forecasts, by definition, will sometimes be right and sometimes be wrong—otherwise we'd call them facts." Craig leaned forward. "You can blame yourselves when your forecasts are wrong, or you can change the way you work so it matters less."

Steve exhaled and slumped back. "How much of that three-hundred-page document will you actually deliver?" Craig asked.

He did the maths. "Maybe eighty pages. Maybe a hundred."

Craig's jaw dropped. "I had no idea."

"Few do." Steve allowed himself a small, defiant smile. "But it is the most important third."

"So why did you build the other two thirds?"

"Scarcity. IT resource is scarce. Customers know they'll only get one shot at a project, so they ask us to build every feature they can think of—even ones they'll never use. My eldest daughter does the same with roasted potatoes. She piles them onto her plate, hoarding them, just in case. We've asked her to just take more later if she wants them."

Craig half-laughed, half-snorted. "She now takes them in smaller batches, based on actual demand?"

Steve's jaw fell open. "Oh, yes. Smaller batches."

"Customers hoard because they don't trust they'll get a second chance. They pad the requirements with features they don't really want, to protect the features they do want."

"Packing peanuts," Steve said, surprising himself.

Craig raised an eyebrow.

"Foam stuffing. To protect what's actually valuable in the box."

Craig grinned. "I like that." He stood and checked his watch. "I need to leave very soon or my son's wedding rehearsal will be ruined and his new mother-in-law will end up in jail for murdering me."

"And then you're not back for three weeks."

"But I have good news. You have all the pieces of the jigsaw in your brain, and I'm certain you can figure this out." He headed for the door, then turned. "Homework: think about how Cheryl's lessons apply to software. Think about small batches. And call Catherine—ask her whether she could have told you what the minimum viable product was right from the start."

He paused. "I'll meet you here at half five tomorrow for an impossibly early breakfast. After that, I'm off the grid."

Craig's phone buzzed. He glanced at the screen. "Eleanor wants to know how we're getting along. What shall I tell her?"

"Like old friends," Steve said.

Craig repeated his words into the phone, then hung up and shook Steve's hand. He'd be on vacation for three weeks. Steve was on his own.

## Home

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve drives home to his kids, his mother, and a plate of mushroom risotto. Ashley negotiates a bedtime story. Alison tells him a joke. And Mom tells him something he doesn't want to hear.*

Steve drove home the way he always did, past the museum where he and Fran used to spend rainy Sunday afternoons, past the estate where his kids played on the swings and the trampoline, past the cemetery. Some days the route was just a route. Other days it was a timeline of everything he'd had and lost.

Today it was both.

He parked and climbed out, pausing to look at the old mansion building in the distance—a habit he'd developed over the years. There was activity on the far side, some sort of event being set up. He slammed the car door and went inside, hungry, tired, and bewildered—but surprisingly enthusiastic about life. What a day he'd had.

Mom was in the kitchen tossing a salad. She'd already put the kids to bed but said Ashley, his seven-year-old, was likely still reading if he wanted to say goodnight. He went upstairs, and before he knew it the goodnight kiss had been negotiated into a kiss, a cuddle, and a chapter from one of her sickly pink fairy books. He gave her a final kiss, tucked her in, and turned out the light.

On the landing, Alison—three years younger than Ashley but still very much awake—shouted, "Night-night, Daddy."

He went in for a cuddle. She told him a joke. "Where does chocolate mousse come from?"

He gave up.

"Chocolate cows!"

She pronounced it *moooooos*. Steve laughed far more than the joke deserved, said night-night, and headed down to the kitchen.

He'd expected Parmesan chicken, but Mom had changed her mind and made mushroom risotto—the no-fuss version, cooked in a non-stick wok, which took far less stirring and tasted just as good. Such was the nature of evening meals. He poured them each a glass of wine and told her about his day while they ate. She listened patiently, nodding in the right places and asking the right questions.

When he'd finished, she said, "How much faith do you have in this Craig fellow?"

"He comes highly recommended by Norbert."

"I don't want you getting your hopes up."

"Yeah. I know."

She reached across and rested her hand on his. "You know, Steven, things might not work out. You don't need the money, but you do need to work. You've always needed to work. It's who you are." She squeezed his hand. "I want you to follow Norbert's direction and consider your other options while the timing is right. If you need to commute to Edinburgh or Glasgow, we'll make that work somehow."

Steve waved the problem away. He wasn't ready to give up on FPP yet. "I'll be okay."

Mom shook her head and made her serious face. "You need to protect yourself and your family. You need to start looking at other options."

He laughed, trying to hide his discomfort. "I could stay at home and look after Alison until she starts school next year. Free up your time a bit."

Her face hardened. "Would you really do that? Really? I know you love your kids enormously, but is that something you'd want?"

He looked down at the table, chastened. They both knew that would never work. He'd go stir-crazy without work.

"In that case," she said, standing, "you'd better do your best to make this FPP thing succeed. And tomorrow, no matter what, you call your friend Graeme." Steve made a face. Graeme worked for a recruitment company in Glasgow. "Call him tomorrow. Wyxcomb will never be as loyal to you as you are to it."

She picked up her plate and went to her apartment—her granny flat—at the back of the house.

Steve started the cleanup.

## Homework at the Kitchen Sink

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve does the dishes and thinks about Cheryl's lessons. His eye wanders around the kitchen and he starts seeing feature bloat everywhere. He realises the real question isn't what to build—it's how to get customers to stop hoarding and start prioritising.*

He and Mom had a deal: she cooked, he cleaned. She was good at cooking and he was not; she didn't like cleaning and he enjoyed it. The kitchen was tiny—old houses, old kitchens—and there was only room for one person at a time.

He cleared the table, flipped through the radio until it found a song he recognised, then went back to the sink and started scrubbing. Within moments he'd switched into autopilot, freeing his mind to replay the day. The mutiny. The cafeteria. Craig's packing peanuts. The very real possibility that he wouldn't have a job in a few months.

Not your typical day at the office.

He'd also been assigned homework—something that hadn't happened in nearly twenty years. Best get on with that.

Like most things in the kitchen, the dish rack was small, and it was soon full. He grabbed a fresh dish towel, quickly did the drying, then started washing again. Small batches of dishes, he thought. Then shook his head. Craig had got inside it.

He grabbed another pot from the stove and dunked it in the soapy water. Mom had used it for tomorrow's porridge, and even though she'd soaked it, the inside was thick with stuck-on oats. Ick.

While he scrubbed, his eye wandered around the kitchen. The scrubbing brush he held did one thing and it did it well. His fridge kept stuff cold, and he didn't want much more from it than that—other than perhaps making a Star Trek *whisssshhh* sound when the doors opened. His microwave was a tiny thing they used to defrost and reheat, though when they first came out they'd been sold as super-dooper oven replacements with all sorts of features nobody used. The remote control with its forest of untouched buttons. The TV—he couldn't tell you what it said on the spec sheet, but he could tell you the mute button was worn out.

Everything in his kitchen was either doing one thing brilliantly or had seventeen features nobody asked for. Craig had rewired his eyes. He was seeing it everywhere now.

"There must be a way to get our customers to prioritise," he said aloud to the dishes. "If I can do that, then I can break the vicious circle."

But how? In the cafeteria, they prioritised by cooking more of what customers were actually buying. Steve couldn't just cook up a batch of features cheaply and make more if they sold well. Software didn't work that way.

He scrubbed at the porridge pot, thinking.

## Small Batches Click

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve finds Ashley's half-eaten cereal—she always takes more than she needs. The insight clicks: small batches are simply small projects delivering the most important features first. Disappointingly dull, but right.*

He went to the lounge looking for stray dishes and found two bowls the kids had left. One was empty, the other half-full of soggy cornflakes. That would be his little Ashley—she always took more cereal than she needed, then they ended up wasting it. He'd asked her many times to just take a small bowl's worth and get more if she was still hungry.

But she was still young.

He took the bowls back to the kitchen and kept thinking.

He'd told Craig that if only their customers prioritised features at the start of each project, they could build a smallish version of the solution—just big enough to deliver comfortably before the deadline. Then, if they had time and budget left over, they could add the next highest features.

That sounded like cooking a medium-sized batch followed by a few smaller ones. Craig had refused to tell him what small batches looked like in software. He said Steve would figure it out.

Could it be something as simple as breaking a big project down into a bunch of smaller projects?

He thought it could.

Was that all there was to it?

He thought a moment, and decided it was. A small batch was a small project in which they delivered a small selection of the *most important* features.

How disappointingly dull. He'd expected thunderclaps and awe, or maybe a eureka moment where he jumped out of the office and ran naked down the street. But there was none of that. Did Cheryl feel similarly underwhelmed when she figured out she shouldn't cook a huge pot of cabbage, but several smaller ones? Probably.

Still, it felt right and it felt good. Steve smiled and said aloud, "Nice."

Small batches were small projects. But that still didn't help him with the prioritisation problem.

Or did it?

They couldn't get customers to prioritise when they did big projects. Would they behave differently if, instead of asking them to lock in twelve months of work, they asked them to commit to just the first month? What if they told Catherine the analysts, designers, developers and testers were ready to start, and if she didn't tell them what to work on, they'd choose on her behalf and start spending her budget?

Yes. Small batches would force their customers to prioritise.

But a key is useless without someone to put it in the lock, turn it, and yank the door open.

He wiped down the benches, gave the kitchen floor a quick sweep, and rushed through to the hallway to grab his phone from his jacket. Fran used to call it Camilla—because sometimes, she said, it was like there were three of them in their relationship. He still smiled every time he picked it up.

# Part Three: The Inverted Pyramid

## The Egyptian Revelation

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve calls Catherine and realises she could have told them the minimum viable product from day one. He thinks about Ron's three-list system. Then he hunts for a book in his attic—and discovers the inverted pyramid.*

Do you know any ex-smokers who only found the impetus to give up after surviving their first heart attack?

Steve knew two.

FPP was in a state of emergency—his equivalent of a heart attack—and he had to make the most of that.

He dialled Catherine. She answered on the tenth ring.

"What else would I, a good-looking single woman in the prime of my life, be doing in the evening?" she said. "Having a social life? Ask away."

"Could you have told us what FPP's minimum viable solution was—the bare bones from the War Room—way back at the start of the project?"

She didn't even pause. "Of course."

"Can you roll forward to December, after FPP is live? Can you tell me what we should be working on next?"

"Sure. My team's put together a spreadsheet." She rattled off features without pausing for breath: online applications, automated statements, the comparison tool, direct debit setup, the web app they'd descoped.

While Catherine talked, Steve thought about how he explained complicated slideshows—revealing concepts piece by piece, building ideas up over time, taking care not to overwhelm his audience. That's how Ron McKnight's support team worked too. They started with an existing system and added to it bit by bit, one change request at a time. Ron was flexible because he kept his work-in-progress short and never made commitments about future work until he could reasonably do so. Small batches by another name.

Catherine continued describing how the system would grow—feature upon feature upon feature—and then the most important piece of the puzzle revealed itself from across the room.

A newspaper sat folded on the couch, front page headline visible above the fold. Something about factory closures. Steve didn't need to read the article to know what it said—that was the clever thing about how reporters wrote. Most important stuff at the top, then the next most important, then the next. It gave them and their editors enormous flexibility.

He thanked Catherine and hung up. Then he went to his bookcase, looking for a book about marketing and communications he'd read years ago. It had a story about the telegraph system.

Mom arrived carrying a small plate of cheese and nuts, in case he was still hungry.

"What are you looking for?"

"A book."

"What book?"

"A book about marketing and communications. It's important."

He checked the first shelf. Not there.

"Some cheese?"

"Maybe in a few minutes." He checked the second shelf.

"Some nuts, then?"

"Maybe later." Third shelf. Plenty of books, but not the right one.

"I may take a lover. Perhaps two," Mom said.

"Uh-hum."

"Younger men, with stamina."

Steve stopped. Gave her his full attention. "What?"

She smiled. She knew she had him. "What's the book called?"

"It's called *Made to Stick*. It's got a story in it about the telegraph system."

"Is it in the attic?"

He dashed up the stairs to the landing, reached up and turned the attic trapdoor latch with his fingertips, and carefully let the door drop open.

Mom followed him up. "Is something wrong?"

"Nothing's wrong. Trust me. Everything is about to fall into place."

She frowned, not understanding.

Steve spoke with his hands emphasising the important bits. "Look, Ron's team works on lots of little projects. He ships new software several times each month. He's got three lists—bug fixes, small enhancements, medium enhancements—and they deliver a constant stream of completed requests. Every few days, *bam!* Another one shipped. Start the next one. Then a few days later—*bam!*—another one."

He could see she wasn't keeping up, but he kept going anyway. He needed to hear the words spoken aloud to sanity-check them.

"When the FPP thing happened, Ron said he could change his release dates just like that, without any hassle. His team is so much more nimble than my project teams. And why? Because his workload is made up of loads of small requests. His team completes each one, tests the arse off it, makes sure they haven't broken anything, and releases it. Sometimes one at a time, sometimes batched up—whatever makes sense. But they never do big batches. Never."

He stopped. "You see?"

She said, "I don't like it when you say the word arse, Steven."

Well, at least she was listening. He apologised, then stood on tiptoes and reached for the attic ladder.

"Be careful. You'll wake the kids."

He nodded and carefully pulled the ladder down. It clicked into place.

"But with FPP, it's a big project, right? It's not made up of loads of little requests. At least, that's what we thought. But we can. At least I think we can."

"I haven't got a clue what you're talking about. Just don't wake the kids. They'll be grumpy in the morning."

Sometimes Steve worried that his mother didn't understand him. Perhaps it was a generational thing. Perhaps it was because he was speaking gibberish.

He climbed into the attic, turned on the light, and picked his way through old PCs, a broken printer, and vacuum-packed bags of baby clothes until he found the boxes of old books. He rooted around until he found what he was looking for.

When he made his way back down the ladder, Mom was still there watching. He flipped through the pages and almost immediately found what he wanted.

That was when Steve had what he would later call his Egyptian Revelation.

He turned the page towards her and smiled. "There it is: the inverted pyramid."

## We Cannot Deliver on December 1st

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Three weeks later. Steve tells the team the truth: December is impossible the way they're working. He introduces the inverted pyramid—deliver the most important features first, each chunk "good enough to ship." Catherine resists, then takes charge.*

*Three weeks later. Early September.*

Catherine clicked the *Finish* button and the cursor turned into an hourglass.

Her PC desktop projected on the conference room wall.

They watched and waited.

Ten of them sat squished around the conference table; another dozen stood against the walls. The room looked more lived in than it had three weeks earlier—walls covered in printouts, sticky notes, and drawings. A large fluorescent pink, green and white striped toy snake, which Vrinda had named Fippy, had mysteriously threaded his way across the top of the projection screen frame. His tongue cast a silhouette on the screen.

Catherine wore a paper hat with CSR written on it in thick black pen. Customer Service Representative. She was pretending to be one—keying the first customer application form into the software. Steve had seen it work twice the day before, but he was still nervous. Their team's confidence, his credibility, their collective future—a lot was riding on this thin slice of FPP software working properly.

\* \* \*

Three weeks earlier, Steve had met Craig for a very early breakfast and explained the inverted pyramid to him. Craig had shrugged and said he knew Steve would come up with something, as long as he was desperate and tried hard not to think about small batches.

Later that morning, Steve had assembled the leadership team.

Phil leaned in close before the meeting started. "There's a rumour going around that Hal had you fired."

Steve shook his head. "Not yet."

He stood and faced the room. "We cannot deliver FPP on December 1st."

Silence. Tim finally answered. "February was a stretch. December is impossible."

Vrinda said, "We are on a Death March."

Catherine looked at Steve expectantly. "But you're working on a plan, right?"

"I've been working with Craig Lally over the last few weeks"—which was, technically, true—"and we've identified a tweak to our existing processes that will increase our odds of succeeding."

"*Increase* our odds?"

"Honestly, I know this will make things better, but I don't know if we'll make the date."

He walked to the wall and picked six sticky notes—Catherine's highest priority features—and handed them to Gregor. "Hypothetically, how long would it take to deliver a product containing only those six features?"

Gregor looked miserable. He studied them, screwed up his face, then shook his head. "I don't really know. We'd need to plan in more detail. Maybe two weeks if we got lucky. Six or seven if we don't."

Steve took all but one sticky note back. "How long for just that one?"

"Look, Steve, this is unfair. There's too much uncertainty. Too many things we can't know until we try."

"But you'd be able to deliver it by December 1st?"

"Almost certainly."

Steve feigned surprise. "You mean definitely?"

"Well, almost definitely then."

Catherine exploded. "Why the hell did you commit to deliver the entire project on February 1st if you can't even tell me how long it takes to deliver one little feature?" She spun to face Steve. "I thought you guys were supposed to be professionals."

"Professionals!" Gregor blurted. "You guys upstairs wouldn't even know about Chaste's ambush if it weren't for Phil. How much money did Mark promise FPP would deliver into Hal's coffers, based on being first to market? How do *you* have the nerve to call *yourselves* professionals?"

"Oh right, blame us for this mess. You made a promise which we took in good faith. You're screwing around with my career here—not to mention Wyx-Fin's reputation and profitability."

Steve intervened. "I made that promise, Catherine, not Gregor. And I admit I've had my fingers crossed since, hoping I'd make good on it. We haven't. I'm sorry."

"Well, I'm sorry too, Steven, but if you can't even commit to delivering that one feature, then I've got to go back to Mark and recommend we scrap the entire frigging project." She sat back, folded her arms, and muttered something Steve's mum wouldn't have approved of.

To Steve's surprise, Vrinda jumped to Gregor's defence. "He didn't say he couldn't deliver that feature. He said he couldn't promise with certainty. Earthquakes, fires, floods—they're very unlikely."

Phil stuck his hand up as if asking permission to speak. Really, he was Steve's plant—they'd discussed this before the meeting. Steve nodded.

"Realistically, it's more likely the messaging technology will blow up in our face."

The whole room turned to stare at him.

"Hey, it's in your risk register. It looked okay during proof-of-concept, but it's never been properly tested. We're guinea pigs."

Catherine folded her arms. "So we won't know if it's an issue until we start testing?"

Tim stepped in. "The project is loaded with question marks. Things we can't know until we test. The messaging system is top of our list."

Catherine unfolded her arms. "That's why we test, right? Then you need to start testing it today."

Steve nodded in what he hoped was a thoughtful manner. "You're right. We must start testing as soon as possible. But we need to test more than just the messaging system. And we can't wait until the testing phase starts."

Phil stood, collected the six sticky notes, and stuck them back on the wall. He stared for a minute, then picked one up.

"We have to test this eventually. And let's be honest, rework it—there's no way it works right the first time. The good news is we *can* pull some testing forward and start now. If it uncovers bad news—good! We've found it early, when there's still time to fix it."

Gregor joined Phil at the wall. He peeled off another sticky. "We should do the same with this one. Nasty mainframe changes that haven't been tested yet."

Soon everyone was standing at the wall, and after fifteen minutes they'd agreed to spend the next week or so testing and reworking three of the riskier high-value features.

The team rejoined the table.

"We'll learn a lot about the quality of our earlier work," Catherine said.

Gregor added, "And in a week or so, we'll have a far better idea whether we can hit December 1st."

Steve nodded. "Agreed. We start the testing phase today, with those features as our first small batch. But there's more to it than that."

He told them about the inverted pyramid—how nineteenth-century journalists invented a new way of writing articles so they could keep their commitments under conditions of high uncertainty. Before the telegraph, reporters wrote introduction-middle-conclusion: the president ate breakfast, went to the theatre, got shot, was rushed to a doctor, and died. But the telegraph was unreliable for long messages. If the conclusion got cut off, the story was useless.

He paused and chuckled, genius-like. No one laughed back.

"If the conclusion is missing, the story loses its point," Catherine said. "It's only useful if transmitted in its entirety."

Steve nodded. "Can you see the connection to FPP?"

"Our product can't ship—which is when it becomes valuable—until all the testing is done. Is that what you mean?"

"Exactly. The reporters solved their problem by inverting their articles. Most newsworthy paragraph at the top, then the next most important, and so on. Even if transmission was interrupted halfway, the editor still had a useful article."

Gregor tilted his head. "Very clever."

Steve let that sink in. "I want us to invert FPP. Deliver the most important paragraph first, then the next, and so on until we hit December 1st. Each paragraph—each small batch—must be good enough to ship."

"*Good enough to ship?*"

"Good enough to ship. We won't ship until December 1st, but I want each small chunk fully tested, fully reworked—good enough quality that we *could* ship it."

Catherine didn't look happy. "So you're reneging on your War Room promise. Is this just another way of descoping?"

"Yes. It's a severe descoping *now* rather than later. By the time we leave this room, I hope to have pared the project back to just those three stickies. They'll be our first chunk of good-enough-to-ship software. We test and rework until it's ready, then start the next chunk."

"But you can't tell me exactly what I'll get on December 1st?"

"Nope. But if we don't change, it's probable you'll get nothing."

Her eyebrows furrowed. "Are you asking me for permission, Steve, or telling me this is how it's going to be?"

Steve made eye contact with everyone around the table. "I'm saying this is how it has to be."

"In that case," Catherine said, "we'd better decide what goes in this chunk, and then the following ones."

Gregor said, "Phil, Tim and I can do that."

Catherine shook her head. "No. I need to be involved."

"Why?"

"I'm not getting every feature on the wall, right?"

Steve said, "It seems unlikely."

"In that case, I must prioritise to ensure we get the best possible product within the time available."

Gregor said, "You've already prioritised. I thought we'd tackle them in order."

She shook her head and went to the wall. She grabbed one of the top three high-risk features—the one that needed mainframe work—and stuck it on the far right. "This is high risk, but it's lower value than other features. I'd rather increase my odds of having the others and find a manual workaround for this one."

She stepped back, then started rearranging stickies. She crumpled one up and replaced it with three others from different parts of the wall.

"We've still got budget until April 1st, right?"

"At least."

"So we can keep working this way after we go live in December?"

Steve hadn't thought that far ahead. "It's your budget."

"In that case"—she moved another bunch of stickies to the right—"I don't need these until shortly after we go live."

The re-planning took another thirty minutes, and Steve sat back and watched as the team restructured the wall. His only contribution was to ask *That would be good enough to ship, right?* every so often. They were sailing into uncharted territory, making this up as they went along. But for the first time in months, the room had energy instead of dread.

In the end, they settled on a tiny chunk—the tip of the iceberg—that would take between one and two weeks to finalise, depending on what ugly issues they uncovered.

## It Worked

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Catherine clicks Finish. The hourglass spins. Transaction complete. The first chunk is good enough to ship—but it took three weeks instead of two. They need to double their speed.*

Three weeks later—Phil's concerns about the messaging system having come true—they finally delivered their first chunk.

Catherine clicked the *Finish* button. The cursor turned into an hourglass.

They watched and waited.

The hourglass disappeared—it felt like minutes but could only have been seconds—and a small dialog box confirmed that the customer's application form had been processed. Catherine clicked Okay, dismissing the dialog, and swapped her CSR hat for one that said CUSTOMER.

"Now, where's my confirmation letter?"

Phil pressed a key on his laptop and the laser printer behind him hummed into life. Gregor had "borrowed" the printer from one of the production support teams. Fairness, as it should, came a distant second to survival.

Catherine compared the confirmation letter with her application form, then nodded her approval. She checked a few more things, then turned to Steve with a serious tone.

"Steven Abernethy, I am delighted with what I have seen. The functionality is severely limited, but it contains our most important features."

Steve thought he heard the room's old-fashioned windows rattle in their frames as everyone breathed out at once. The room came alive with mutual back-slapping.

"But," said Catherine, no longer smiling, "this chunk took three weeks, when we predicted one or two. We took twenty percent of our remaining time to deliver ten percent of the scope. We need to double our speed, and then some. Can we?"

Tim stood. "This first chunk was always going to be the slowest and the most unpredictable. We've fixed a lot of serious underlying problems which now we won't have to fix again. And we've been learning to work this new way." He paused. "The first few attempts when you learn to ride a bike are always wobbly. We will speed up."

Catherine bit her lip. "But you can't guarantee we'll have a commercially viable product by December?"

Gregor shrugged. "I honestly don't know. In another week, we'll know more. A week after that, we'll know even more."

"Okay," Catherine said, looking sad. "Can you track those two percentages over time? Put them on a chart so everyone can see?"

He said he would.

Steve thanked everyone for their hard work, and the room slowly emptied.

When just the two of them remained, Catherine said, "I just wish a month had already passed and we had a better idea of how fast we could run."

"I know."

Steve wished that Craig, who had inspired all of this, could have been there. But he was still on vacation.
