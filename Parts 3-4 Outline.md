# Parts 3 & 4 Outline — Proposed Structure

## Where We Are

Part Two Working Draft is complete. It ends with "It Worked" — the first chunk of GETS software is approved, but Catherine warns they're running at half the speed they need. Craig is still on vacation. The team has the inverted pyramid but they don't know if they can go fast enough to hit December 1st.

The remaining story (original chapters 36–61) covers:
- Eleanor's validation and challenge
- The bottleneck discovery and application
- Catherine's descoping breakthrough and the "slam dunk"
- The run into launch
- The job offer and resolution

This outline proposes two final parts:
- **Part Four: Transformation Under Fire** (chapters 36–58)
- **Part Five: Resolution** (chapters 59–61)

---

## The Problem with Summarising These Chapters

Chapters 36–61 contain the book's **best character moments**, its **most important teaching**, and its **emotional climax**. The temptation is to rush through them because there's a lot of plot. That's backwards. The plot is straightforward — team speeds up, scope shrinks, deadline met, Steve gets a job offer, chooses family. What makes these chapters worth reading is the *way* they happen: Eleanor picking Kryz as her prey, Cheryl's china bottleneck, Steve muttering "I am awesome" to himself on a hill, Catherine's "No way! No way! No way!", Mom making bacon whether anyone asked for it.

**The rule from Part Two applies here too: compress teaching, not charm.**

---

## Key Structural Decisions

### 1. The bottleneck must be taught through Cheryl, not Gregor
The original has Gregor mention Theory of Constraints from his MBA (Ch 43). This is redundant in the condensed version — Cheryl teaches it better, with more charm, and Gregor's contribution adds nothing the reader doesn't already get from the Mahjella and China stories. Cut Gregor's TOC chapter entirely.

### 2. Catherine's descoping needs to LAND, not be summarised
In the original, Catherine's slam dunk happens across three chapters (44, 45, 46). The details of individual throughput improvements (seven ideas in Ch 45) can be summarised in a paragraph. But the moment Steve secretly withholds the speed improvement to maintain Catherine's desperation, and the moment she discovers it ("You arse"), are the best beats in the middle act. These must be fully dramatised.

### 3. Chapters 50–58 must be heavily compressed
The original spends nine chapters on: time skip, Ron's wisdom, Catherine's customer-level income, the web app, the messaging crisis, Catherine drowning, pig/ribbon, internal launch, and walk before you run. That's a LOT of ground. Most of it is plot mechanics and secondary teaching. The condensed version needs to keep:
- Ron's "you didn't invent it" humbling
- Catherine's "I'm not begging for a change" paradigm shift
- The Riverdance launch and live reveal
- Walk Before You Run (baby Hamish)

And compress or cut:
- The web app origin story (one line, not a full chapter)
- The messaging crisis (one paragraph of tension)
- The seven throughput ideas (one paragraph summary)
- Catherine's drowning (fold into the launch sequence)

### 4. The gravity line MUST be restored
"Gravity didn't always suck" is the book-title payoff. The original gives it a whole chapter (47) — Steve alone on Kelvin's Peak, eating a cheese baguette, flicking a pebble off the cliff. The condensed version doesn't need a full chapter. It needs **one perfect beat** — probably 3–5 lines at the end of the slam dunk chapter, or as a standalone micro-chapter.

### 5. The resolution deserves its own part
Chapters 59–61 are the emotional climax of the book — not the business climax (that's the launch in Ch 57–58) but the human one. The job offer, the evaporating cloud applied to Steve's own life, the bacon sandwich, Scooby-Doo, and "Fran would be proud of you." These need space and stillness. They shouldn't feel like an epilogue tacked on; they should feel like the reason the book exists.

## Proposed Chapter Structure

### Part Four: Transformation Under Fire

#### 1. The Lioness (~800 words)
**Original:** Chapters 36–37 (Eleanor's visit + finding bugs without testing)

Eleanor arrives. Ignores Steve's carefully prepared demo. Charges across the floor and ambushes a cross-functional team at their desks — "a hungry lioness picking out an antelope for dinner." She picks Krzysztof, clearly the skinniest antelope.

**Key beats to keep:**
- "I'm quite sure Krzysztof knows his own name, Steven."
- Steve scanning the room for emergency exits
- Kryz's candid report: first chunk horrible, second better, third even better. "This is the first time analysts, developers and testers have worked together as a team."
- Catherine's prunes analogy ("like force-feeding our department a pile of prunes. We finally got things flowing")
- Craig appears beside Steve — beard longer, face tanned. Quick handshake, thumbs-up, mouths "Good"
- Phil's exaggerated hunger mime from the conference room window
- The warm sausage rolls / cold sandwiches gag
- **Kryz's discovery: finding bugs without testing.** Three of them sit together now. Sharon spun around in her chair and tapped them both on their shoulders. They talk about expected behaviour *before* testing. If they disagree, the spec was ambiguous, and the developer probably built the wrong thing.
- **The coffee shop ambiguity joke.** Phil recommended an Italian cafe. Kryz tried it, emailed thanks. Phil replied: "Once you try their coffee, you'll never go back." Kryz was confused — he intended to go back. Eleanor snarfs.
- Eleanor's fury turns on Steve: "What I do not understand is why you did not have these conversations months ago! How much rework could you have prevented?"
- Craig's graceful exit after being dismissed
- Eleanor cancels the demo. Wants Steve and Gregor alone.

**Why this matters:** This is Steve's worst public humiliation — Eleanor dresses him down because his team innovated *without his knowing*. But it's also the Three Amigos idea (preventing defects by talking before testing), which is one of the book's core practical takeaways. And the coffee shop joke is one of the most memorable moments in the novel.

#### 2. Mom Makes Him Call Craig (~600 words)
**Original:** Chapters 38–39 (crisis meeting compressed + Mom's kitchen)

**Open with Eleanor's crisis meeting (compressed to ~150 words):**
- "Will you deliver FPP on December 1st? Yes or No?" Just Steve, Gregor, Eleanor.
- Steve: "We might." Eleanor: "Do you have a plan or are you clueless?" Steve: "The second option."
- Eleanor's chin quivers. "Halifax and I have made a very big promise to some very important people."
- Plan by Monday evening
- After Eleanor leaves: "There goes our weekends." Steve: "I was booked to take my kids to Euro Disney this weekend. It was meant to be a surprise." Gregor: "Lucky they don't know what they're missing." Steve: "I stopped making promises to my kids a long time ago. It wasn't fair."
- Gregor mentions Plan A (lower quality standards, ship non-GETS). Steve shakes his head. "But, as a last resort..."

**Cut to Sunday kitchen (~450 words):**
- 4:52 p.m. Mom: "You look like a bag of bums, Steven."
- Steve: spent all weekend with Gregor and spreadsheets. Can't make December 1st. "We'd have to run at least twice as fast."
- Smiley face pizzas on a baking tray
- Cat named Purronimo (just called him Mo). Rubs the back of his neck.
- Mom: "What did Craig say?" Steve mumbles. Mom: "He's got a phone, hasn't he?"
- Steve does a teenager-like one-shoulder shrug and grunts "S'pose."
- "A man stopped, admitted he was lost, and asked for directions for the second time in the same calendar year."
- Craig's email back: **"Where is FPP's bottleneck?"**
- Mom: "Where is it?" Steve: "I don't know." Mom: "Then email him back and tell him you don't know."
- Craig's longer reply: you've turned your teams into a test-scenario processing factory. Find your bottleneck. Read *The Goal*. "Ask Cheryl to show you her bottleneck." PS: off scuba diving and canyoning. Signed half a dozen waiver forms because of his age.
- Steve downloads the ebook. Mom forces him to buy the audiobook on double-speed.
- Falls asleep on the couch. Wakes at 1:30 a.m. Someone has removed his glasses, unplugged his earphones, and placed a blanket over him.

**Why this matters:** Mom is the engine of this chapter. She makes Steve call Craig, email Craig, read the book. Without her, he'd have sulked. The blanket detail is wordless love. And the Euro Disney line is devastating because the kids don't even know what they missed.

#### 3. China (~700 words)
**Original:** Chapter 40 (Breakfast with Cheryl / the bottleneck)

**Key beats:**
- 6:17 a.m. "You're not one of them vegetarians, are you?" Bacon and egg breakfast. Lavazza coffee — Pavarotti's favourite. Cheryl peels a mandarin.
- The water bottle analogy: "The skinniest part at the top determines how fast stuff flows."
- **Mahjella's story.** Five steps on Cheryl's left hand. 100/hour, 80, 120, 50, thousands. Steve points to the fourth finger. "If you made any other step faster, would the department process any more invoices?" "No." Cheryl slaps the table: "Exactly!"
- Mahjella was the bottleneck — the only person authorised for step 4, spending half her day apologising to suppliers instead.
- Solution: Mahjella locks herself away and focuses. Team pre-checks invoices. Within a week: backlog cleared.
- "We all know the secret to life is focus. But that only works if you know *where* to focus."
- **"Where was your bottleneck, Cheryl?" "China." "Where?" "Not where. *What*."**
- Kitchen tour: not enough clean plates during peak hours. Servers say fresh batch in a few minutes. Queue builds. Customers lost.
- "I couldn't ask for more money, so I tried to make the bottleneck more efficient." The conveyor dishwasher: 8 seconds, water so hot it evaporates. Switched from big batches to small batches of plates.
- Whack-A-Mole: next bottleneck was tills, then seats. Adding takeout and delivery.
- "We used to be a disliked cost centre. Nowadays we are a much-liked profit centre."
- Beautiful morning. Steve calls Phil. "What's up, dude?"

**Why this matters:** This is the core bottleneck lesson. Everything that follows — Phil's analysis, Catherine's slam dunk, the testing speed-up — depends on this scene. Cheryl teaches it physically, concretely, with mandarin segments and kitchen tours. The China misdirect is the comedic highlight.

#### 4. This Is Awesome (~500 words)
**Original:** Chapter 41 (Phil at Kelvin's Peak)

**Key beats:**
- Kelvin's Peak. Two coffees. Phil's T-shirt: "I wish I could change the world, but they won't give me the source code."
- Polite insults (friendship in this part of the world)
- Phil: "Do you think the bottleneck concept applies to us?" Steve: complicated, it's a project not a factory... Phil: "None of that matters. It's still information flow. We must have a bottleneck."
- Five candidates: analysts, testers, developers, managers, customers. Phil: "Could also be build machines or test environments. Now zip it old man, and let me think."
- Phil closes his eyes. Thinks. And thinks. Steve enjoys the view.
- Phil: "Three roles can't be the bottleneck." Not analysts (analysis mostly done). Not customers. "And we ain't got no shortage of managers."
- Developers or testers? In the first chunk: definitely developers. But now: fixing defects at about the same rate they find them. Testing is becoming the bottleneck.
- "No, no, no. It's worse than I thought." Regression testing will explode with every new batch.
- Phil's eyes widen. **"This is awesome! If we squeeze a twenty percent improvement out of testing, our entire project runs twenty percent faster!"**
- Steve: "Rather than figure out how to make everyone more efficient, we only need to figure out how to make our testers more efficient."
- Phil: "I need to slow my developers down." Steve: "*What?*" Phil: "Would you prefer they keep fixing bugs we can't retest? All that does is build up a pile of half-done work."
- Agreement: focus all improvement on testers. Cancel non-essential meetings. More frequent code builds. Roll out Kryz's pre-test discussions. Find things developers can automate for testers.

**Why this matters:** The bottleneck application to FPP is the central mechanism for the rest of the book. Phil's "This is awesome!" is infectious and correct — knowing where to focus changes everything. And slowing developers down (the non-bottleneck subordinating to the bottleneck) is the most counterintuitive and memorable teaching beat.

#### 5. The Slam Dunk (~900 words)
**Original:** Chapters 42–46 (compressed)

**Catherine slashes scope (~300 words):**
- Catherine in Steve's office, arms crossed. Chaste press release: they're launching December 1st too. "There are no surprises. Similar offerings, different markets."
- Steve delivers the bad news: can't make December 1st. She knew. "We're not even going to make it, are we?"
- Catherine filters Gregor's spreadsheet. Slashes test-heavy scope. Steve: "How can you do that when you've already cut to bare bones?" Catherine: "Things change. I can't speed your team up, but maybe I can shorten the journey."
- Surname-only search. Steve: "Just one field? Which field?" Catherine: "Surname." Steve: "You're nuts!" Gregor: "That's what I said."
- Catherine's logic: five thousand customers in December, worst case a few dozen Smiths. "Then, in January, I'll ask my staff what fields they desperately need next."
- She keeps slicing. Slashes a fifth of test scenarios. Proposes launching without statements (Day Two feature). Proposes "demand-based" features: deploy automation only if manual process breaks. "You want to wing it?" Catherine: "In a disciplined and planned way."

**Steve's secret (~300 words):**
- Phil and Tim report throughput improvements: ~50% more testing capacity from disbanding the automated testing team, developer-tester pairing, reducing duplicate testing.
- Steve runs Catherine's changes through the spreadsheet WITHOUT adding the speed improvement. Result: slim chance of December 1st.
- **He deliberately doesn't tell them.** He knows if he does, Catherine will stop slicing. He maintains the pressure.
- Vrinda: "We've now got good odds!" Catherine drops her head, closes her eyes. "This isn't good enough. December 1st needs to be a slam dunk."
- More slicing. Customer segmentation. Launch only in Scottish branches. Only to existing customers.

**The reveal (~300 words):**
- Steve asks for Catherine's password. Bumps the speed by fifty percent.
- Catherine watches. **"You arse. You lied to us."**
- Steve clicks the macro.
- **Slam dunk.** Catherine's jaw falls open. "No way! No way! No way!" Vrinda: "We've made it? We've really made it?" Steve nods.
- Steve's internal note: "For the first time in my career, one of my customers had requested we work to a fixed budget, variable scope promise."

**Why this matters:** This is the highest-energy moment in the book's middle act. The combination of Catherine's commercial brilliance (slashing scope in ways the tech team never would have) and Steve's cunning (withholding the speed news to extract more slashing) produces a result neither could have achieved alone. "You arse" is the perfect line — anger and admiration in two words.

#### 6. Gravity (~150 words)
**Original:** Chapter 47

Steve climbs Kelvin's Peak for the second time that day. Brain buzzing. He sits on a rock near the cliff edge, eats a cheese baguette, turns his face to the sun, closes his eyes. Hockey shouts below, seagulls above.

He picks up a pebble and flicks it over the edge. Watches it bounce its way down into the brush at the bottom.

**"Gravity didn't always suck."**

He pulls out his phone and dials Eleanor.

**Why this matters:** This is the book-title payoff. One hundred and fifty words is enough. The silence, the pebble, the line. It earns its place.

#### 7. Calibrating Confidence (~600 words)
**Original:** Chapters 48–49 (Eleanor's betting + four million pounds)

**Eleanor's bets:**
- "Will you take a hundred pound bet against me that you'll go live on December 1st?" "Of course."
- "One thousand?" A nice new television. "Of course."
- "Ten thousand?" Serious money. "If Fran were still alive, she would get very grumpy if ten grand suddenly disappeared." Steve: "I'd take that bet."
- "That's what I wanted to hear. Honestly, Steven? I didn't think you had it in you."
- Steve: "Gee thanks, Eleanor."
- On hold, waiting for Eleanor. Puts phone on speaker. Says aloud: **"Steve, you are awesome."** Just because he was alone and he could.
- Hal: "Abernethy. Is it true? We will ship on December 1st?" Steve: "Yes." Hal: "Do you have any doubts?" Steve: "None."
- Hal: "Is it worthy of the Wyx-Fin brand?" Steve: "I'd buy the product. And I'd recommend my friends buy it too."
- Hal: **"Maybe there is hope..."** (barely audible before the hold music returns)

**Four million pounds:**
- A seagull squawks. Steve throws it bread. Names it Hal. Waits for it to poop on his head.
- Eleanor's financial tutorial: eight months of extra selling × half a million per month = four million pounds. More than Wyx-Fin's entire annual profit.
- Steve plays dumb: "Wow. Two and a half million?" Eleanor: "Are you serious?" Steve: "No. And who'd have thought that a drunken confession from a former employee would bring four million into the company?"
- Eleanor: "Halifax thinks it was his inspirational leadership."
- Steve: "Hal is happy with me?" Eleanor: "As far as he's concerned, pulling the date forward was just good management on his part."
- **"You're playing with the big boys and girls now, Steve. Get over it."** She hangs up.
- **"The seagull had pooped on me, after all."**

**Why this matters:** The betting sequence tests gut conviction, not spreadsheets — a real decision science technique. The four million pounds lesson is the book's most important business insight: the value of delivering early isn't saving development costs (the team keeps working), it's the REVENUE from extra months of selling. And Hal the Seagull is comedy perfection.

#### 8. You Should Have Asked for Help Earlier (~500 words)
**Original:** Chapters 50–51 (time skip + Ron McKnight)

**Time skip (one paragraph):**
Three weeks later. Steve took the kids to the Canary Islands. Read *The Goal* and re-read *Made to Stick*. Back at work: bottleneck improvements bedding in. Flowmasters. Visual whiteboards. Things flowing. Then an email from Eleanor: **"How do you know your software is GETS, considering you haven't shipped anything yet?"** Ten seconds later, Steve is on his way to Ron McKnight's office.

**Ron at 8:36 p.m.:**
- Typing in the dark, face lit green by his mainframe screen. "Just a moment." Looks up, smile drops when he sees Steve. "Steven."
- Ron already knows about FPP. Eleanor asked him to run an independent assessment. His three-word verdict: **"They're fine."**
- Steve: "That's two words." Ron: **"Technically, 'they're' is two words, Steve."**
- Steve: "But... my inverted pyramid approach... it already exists?"
- Ron laughs. **"You inverted the pyramid, but you didn't invent it. You rediscovered it. And renamed it."**
- "You want proof? Lift my monitor." Ron pulls a thick blue book: *Software Engineering Economics*, Barry Boehm, 1981. Sub-heading: "Incremental Development."
- Ten minutes at Ron's whiteboard: ship into live environments now. If things go smoothly, good. If not, even better — find problems with runway ahead.
- Steve: "I should have thought to do this earlier." Ron: **"Wrong. You should have asked for help earlier."**

**Why this matters:** Ron is the gruff hidden genius. The monitor-lifting moment is physically comic and intellectually humbling. "You should have asked for help earlier" lands as a life lesson, not just project advice. And the Boehm book under the monitor — proof that simple isn't the same as easy.

#### 9. I'm Not Begging for a Change (~400 words)
**Original:** Chapter 52 (Catherine's customer-level income)

- Catherine and Gregor arrive, arms crossed. Catherine first asks for a web app for December. Steve and Gregor both say no. She presses. Still no. "Okay. I give up."
- Then: "There is one more thing." She opens Gregor's spreadsheet and adds a new row: Customer Level Income. The change request Gregor has rejected seventeen times.
- Catherine: **"I'm not requesting a change, Steve. I'm not even begging. I'm simply reprioritising our list."**
- Steve searches for a reason to say no. Can't find one. She's right. In the inverted pyramid, the customer owns the priority list.
- Gregor: "We can absorb this, but not this *and* the web app." Catherine smiles sweetly.
- Steve recognises what just happened: "For the first time, one of my customers was using our inverted pyramid approach as a weapon against us."
- Catherine: "Even if the new estimate is five times the old one, I still want it. This will differentiate ours from Chaste's."
- Handshake.

**Why this matters:** This is the moment Catherine masters the new system. She doesn't beg, doesn't negotiate — she simply uses the rules Steve built. It's a genuine paradigm shift in how customers and developers work together. And it changes FPP's competitive position against Chaste.

#### 10. Walk Before You Run (~1,000 words)
**Original:** Chapters 54–58 (compressed)

This chapter merges the messaging crisis, Catherine drowning, pig/ribbon, internal launch, and walk before you run into one propulsive sequence. The original spends five chapters on this; the condensed version needs one.

**The crisis (~150 words):**
- October. The messaging system works in development but not production. Video conference: Phil in the War Room (looking smelly and tired), Steve in a hotel room in Malmö, Norbert at home in Copenhagen.
- Phil: "We have to fly the vendor's specialists in from Australia."
- Norbert: "Fly them first class." And: kick off Plan B (revert to old technology). "Not ASAP. Immediately."
- Ron's team quietly begins ripping out the messaging system. Just in case.

**Catherine's pressure transfer (~200 words):**
- Catherine and Steve at lunch. Dark rings under her eyes. "My get-up-and-go has not only got up and gone, it's taken my will to live with it."
- "No one expected you to launch on time. All that pressure you were under — it's just slid off your shoulders and landed on mine."
- She's pared her call centre scripts back to bare bones. "If we were desperate, we could go live today with what we've got."
- She wants to launch in just one branch. Problem: "When you inverted FPP's feature list, you looked like a hero. If I do the same with our branch rollout, I'll look like the clumsy oaf who dropped the ball."
- Steve: "We need to wrap a ribbon around this pig."
- Mark's router example: "Automatic Network Healing" = reboots itself at 3 a.m. "That wasn't the work of a developer. That was the work of a marketer. Or a psychopath."

**The internal launch (~400 words):**
- November 17th. Basement auditorium. Darkness. Then: the boom of a hundred Irish tap shoes. Riverdance.
- Spotlight on Mark Richmond: top hat and tails, gripping a megaphone "like James Bond with a pistol." "Ladies and Gentlemen — Future Perfect."
- He opens empty fists, springs into the air, snatches the application form from the screen. Hands it to Catherine with a bow. Audience erupts. All except Hal.
- Hal: "Smoke and mirrors? Is that all you've got?" Not in the script.
- Mark: "Enough with the sizzle. Let's talk about the steak."
- Catherine demonstrates the live system. Asks "Mr. Steven J. Abernethy" to join her. Hands him a hat: "S. Abernethy, Customer." He logs in, clicks through, steps back.
- Catherine: "Typical IT guy. You forgot to log out."
- Hal: "Confirm there will be no technical hitches on December 1st." Steve chokes.
- Catherine saves him: "Let me answer that." Steve Abernethy is their seventeenth live customer. *Live.* Last week, he filled out a real application in a real branch. "What you've seen was not a demonstration. It was very, very real."
- The messaging fix came through the previous week. FPP is running in production.
- Mark, megaphone: **"FPP is alive and kicking two weeks ahead of schedule!"**
- Collective gasp. Clapping. Eleanor jabs Steve with her elbow. Mouths "nice."

**Walk Before You Run (~250 words):**
- Hal: "Why this timid drip-drip-drip rollout?"
- Catherine's slide: baby Hamish, eighteen months, holding the edge of a couch. **WALK BEFORE YOU RUN.**
- "We're learning to walk so that when we need to run, in April, we can."
- Second slide: pit crew. "Although our product is live, we've not yet finished developing it. We're using the staggered rollout to test with real staff and real customers. When we find something we don't like, we fix it."
- Mark: "There's nothing like the sound of cash registers ringing to confirm your customers want your product."
- Hal stands. Nods to Mark and Catherine. Finds Steve in the back row. Nods.
- "Your team has done excellent work. Though, frankly, your website is **pig-ugly**."
- Steve smiles. Four weeks ago they didn't have a web app.
- Steve's private thought: "Few of them knew what we'd achieved. None knew I'd soon be rolling out these lessons to other projects."

**Why this matters:** This is the PUBLIC CLIMAX. The live reveal — "What you've seen was not a demonstration" — is the single most satisfying moment in the book. Baby Hamish grounds the grand strategy in a toddler learning to walk. And pig-ugly is as close to a compliment as Hal gets.

### Part Five: Resolution

#### 11. The Dream Job (~600 words)
**Original:** Chapters 59–60

**The job offer (~350 words):**
- Room empties. Hal hops up the aisle, surprisingly agile. Sits beside Steve. "That went far better than I expected."
- Hal: "Norbert is telling everyone you're a superhero and inverting is your secret weapon. I've asked my mother to knit you a Christmas jumper with a great big red I on the front."
- "What do you think, Hal?" Eyes narrow: "You got lucky. Lucky to be managing the wrong project at the right time."
- Background: if FPP doesn't sell well by next August, Wyx-Fin gets sold. Steve out of a job, most staff over time.
- Eleanor: "Norbert wants you, come January, to help others across the Group invert their projects. It will require more travel."
- Apart from the travel, it sounded like the job of his dreams. "It wasn't the job I'd been aspiring to — it was better."
- "We'd prefer you stay. At least until August." Hal: "Think of it as preventing the sale of the company and saving your friends' jobs."
- "Decision by Wednesday."
- Steve: "Wait." They stop. He held all the cards. **"Sit."** They sat.

**The evaporating cloud (~250 words):**
- Steve closes his eyes. Lets his head fall back.
- He silently performs a conflict cloud on himself, physically. Squeezes his right hand: take the new job — an awesome, influential role. Wriggles his right shoulder. Squeezes his left hand: stay — save friends' jobs, more time with family. Wriggles his left shoulder. Tips his head: his and his family's happiness.
- "I was torn between my own personal ambitions and the happiness of my friends and family."
- Phil's old bacon joke. "But that did nothing for my mom or my kids, who bore the brunt of my travel."
- **"Bacon can't solve every problem. Or can it?"**
- He opens his eyes. The pig is the increased travel. But what's the value of being important if his family suffers?
- He smiles. "I had the pig, and in that instant I figured out what the ribbon was."
- Pulls out his phone. Dials Norbert. Switches to speaker. Rests it on the bench top.
- "Hello, Steve. How did your launch go?" Steve: "Great. We need to talk."

**Why this matters:** This is the THEMATIC CLIMAX. Steve applies every tool he's learned — the conflict cloud, the pig and ribbon, the inverted pyramid — to the most important decision of his life. The physical cloud enactment (squeezing, wriggling) makes the abstract tool concrete and personal. He's choosing family, but finding a way to have everything.

#### 12. Bacon Sandwich (~600 words)
**Original:** Chapter 61

- Hungover morning. Regretting "just one pint." Started with Guinness, ambled to an Indian, Kingfisher beer, black Russians.
- Alison under two chairs covered in rugs, watching cartoons. Steve crouches for a hug. **"Your breath stinks, Daddy."** Kisses her anyway. Tickles her.
- Steve: "What you watching?" Alison: "Scooby-Doo." Steve: "Is this the one where they discover the monster isn't really a monster, it's just someone dressed up?" Alison scrunches her nose: **"Yeaah. They're all like that, Dad."** (The monster of the impossible deadline was just someone dressed up.)
- Kitchen. Mom doing The Times crossword. Takes one look at him. "My poor boy." Starts making a bacon sandwich, whether he wanted one or not.
- "You're far too old for these late nights."
- Steve, grinning: **"I got a promotion yesterday."**
- He tells the story leaning in the kitchen doorway while Mom turns bacon, listens, nods. She reaches the bit where he called Norbert just as she finishes slicing the sandwiches in half diagonally — "which is how he liked them when he was a kid."
- Mom: "Does this mean your travel is going to increase?" She looks pained.
- "No."
- "But after next summer?"
- "No."
- "No travel? Tell me then, clever clogs, how did you manage that?"
- He picks up his sandwich. Takes a bite. The butter and the salt, the soft bread and the crunch of the bacon, all mixed together with the ketchup's sharp sweetness.
- "Mmmm. This is so good."
- "Steven?"
- He puts it down. Smiles. "I brokered a deal." He stays in Watt's Bridge. Others fly to him, not the other way round.
- Mom: "Wonderful! Hal and Eleanor get what they want. You get the job you've always wanted. Norbert gets what he wants."
- Steve looks out the window. Alison and Ashley are playing together in the back yard. He watches them a moment. Takes another bite.
- **"He really did love the taste of bacon, but if he ever had to, he thought he could live without it."**
- Steve: **"And, best of all, I get to spend more time at home, with you and the kids."**
- Mom looks down at her hands, then back up. **"Fran would be proud of you."**
- **"That was the best thing anyone could ever say to him."**

**Why this matters:** This is the ending. Every image earns its place. The bacon sandwich comes full circle — Chapter 1 opens with Steve alone eating bacon at 5:26 a.m.; this chapter closes with Mom making him bacon while his children play in the garden. The Scooby-Doo monster is the deadline that was never as impossible as it seemed. And "Fran would be proud of you" is the single most emotionally devastating and uplifting line in the book.

## Key Principles for Writing (same as Part Two, plus additions)

1. **Show scenes, don't summarise them.** Especially: the coffee shop joke, Cheryl's China tour, Catherine's "No way!", the Riverdance reveal, the bacon sandwich.
2. **Keep character moments even if they don't advance the plot.** The blanket on the couch. Hal the Seagull. "Steve, you are awesome." The diagonal sandwich. These ARE the story.
3. **Compress teaching, not charm.** The three amigos concept can be taught in a few lines of Kryz's dialogue. The bottleneck can be taught through Mahjella and china without explaining TOC formally. The seven throughput ideas can be one paragraph.
4. **Steve resists, then changes.** He argues with Phil about slowing developers down. He's annoyed at Eleanor's condescension. He's stunned when Catherine uses his own system against him. He's torn by the job offer. Active, not passive.
5. **Fresh prose, same beats.** No 20+ word chunks from the original. Write new sentences that hit the same emotional and narrative beats. Third person, snappier, faithful.
6. **Keep the humour.** The coffee shop joke. China/Where?/What. "You're nuts!" / "That's what I said." "You arse." Hal the Seagull. "Technically, 'they're' is two words." Riverdance. "Pig-ugly." "Your breath stinks, Daddy." Scooby-Doo. These are the reason people tell their friends about this book.
7. **The ending needs silence.** After the Riverdance spectacle and Hal's grudging approval, the resolution must slow down. The Dream Job and Bacon Sandwich are quiet chapters. No rushing. Let the bacon sizzle.
