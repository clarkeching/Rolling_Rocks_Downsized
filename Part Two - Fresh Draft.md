# Part Two: Out of Moves

> [!info] The Story So Far
> *Pauline's betrayal set off a chain reaction. First, Steve's team scrambled to match Chaste's April 1st launch. Then CEO Halifax Gibbet promised to beat them - pulling the deadline forward to February 1st. Then Chaste moved their own date to February. And Hal, never one to back down, issued a press release announcing December 1st.*
>
> *Steve has pulled every lever he knows: slashed features, cancelled the web app, pushed overtime, cut quality standards. His team mutinied. His staff don't believe the date is achievable. His boss has threatened his job.*
>
> *Craig Lally, the quality guy from upstairs, offered to help him work differently. He drew Steve's dilemma on a napkin, took him to the cafeteria kitchen to meet Cheryl, and showed him two things: the shape of his own trap, and a story about soup that nobody tasted. Steve was polite, but unconvinced. Nice story. Wrong industry. He thanked Craig and left.*
>
> *He's out of moves.*

## Fowl Mood

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve chickens out of confronting Hal. He tells Gregor to undo the damage - cancel the compulsory overtime, reinstate the holidays. Gregor asks the question Steve can't answer: "What would you have done differently?"*

*Wednesday, August 16th. Late afternoon.*

Steve was in a fowl mood when he made it back to his desk. Yes, *fowl*, not foul. Somewhere between the second and fourth floors, the rational part of his brain clicked in. He turned into a big chicken and pushed the button for the sixth floor instead of the penthouse. He'd spent almost twenty years climbing the Wyxcomb ladder, and he wasn't about to throw that all away impulsively.

Instead of giving Hal an excuse to dump him, he summoned Gregor to his office and told him to undo it. All of it. No compulsory overtime. Holidays reinstated.

Gregor didn't argue. He didn't even look surprised. He just asked one question.

"What would you have done differently?"

Steve opened his mouth, then closed it again.

"If you were in my shoes," Gregor said. "What would you have done differently?"

Steve took a deep breath and did the only thing he could. He apologised. Gregor was right. He'd done exactly what Steve would have done - exactly what anyone in his position would have done. And worse, Steve was doing exactly what his predecessors would have done in his situation. He told Gregor that.

Gregor shrugged. "Taking the blame is just part of my job."

Steve shook his head. "But it shouldn't be. We should be better at this."

They left it at that. Gregor headed back to give the team the news, and Steve sat down at his desk to figure out what to do next.

He didn't figure out anything. He scrolled through his emails until he got to one from Hal's personal assistant asking *him* to look at *her* printer because it was jamming a lot. He shook his head, swore briefly under his breath, and called the help desk manager.

Then he sat there, staring at the napkin he'd pulled from his jacket pocket. Five boxes. Two at the bottom, two in the middle, one at the top. Arrows linking them together. An X between the two things he needed most.

Darth stared back at him.

## Start Looking for a New Job

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve calls Norbert, who delivers brutal news: he can't protect Steve if FPP fails. Norbert's advice: beg Craig Lally for help - "Maybe he can see things the rest of us can't." And start looking for a new job.*

He tried Eleanor first. Her PA answered. Eleanor was mid-flight to Sweden with Hal.

So he called Norbert.

Norbert answered immediately and without saying hello. "Can you deliver FPP on December 1st?"

"Realistically?"

"Of course, realistically. We are not playing computer games. You do not have three lives to play with. I ask you again: will you deliver or not?"

Steve didn't know what to say.

"Yes or no. Quick."

"No."

"That's what I thought." The phone went silent for a long moment. "I don't know if I can help you. If FPP doesn't ship, then Hal is gone. It will be difficult for me to protect you under those circumstances."

He may as well have slapped Steve across the face.

Norbert wasn't just his boss. He was the man who'd pulled every bureaucratic string and corporate lever necessary to transfer Steve from Glasgow to Watt's Bridge after Fran died - nearer his home, nearer his mother, nearer the kids. Eleanor hadn't wanted him. She'd wanted someone with smaller ambitions. Norbert had insisted. *In Wyxcomb,* he said, *we look after our own.*

If Norbert couldn't protect him, nobody could. Norbert knew the job market in Watt's Bridge for people like Steve was limited. He knew about the kids, about his mother, about the fact that commuting to Edinburgh or Glasgow was difficult and relocating was worse.

Steve let his breath out. "I need your help, Norbert."

Norbert said nothing.

Steve waited a few seconds, then asked, "What should I do?"

"You talked to your quality guy, Lally. Tell me how that went."

Steve chose his words carefully. "He drew some diagrams. Took me to the cafeteria to show me something. Interesting stuff, but I couldn't see how it applied to what we do."

"I spoke to him. He said you'd had a useful conversation but that you seemed reluctant to take things further."

"When I pressed him, he said he was certain he could help you, but that you'd have to ask for help if you wanted it."

"Oh."

"My advice is for you to ask for his help. Beg if you have to, but get his help."

Steve bit down on his lip. "But why him?"

"Because frankly, I don't know anyone else who wouldn't do precisely what you've already done. You've played the standard playbook, and it's not working. I am clutching at straws here. Maybe he can see things the rest of us can't."

Steve said, "Fresh eyes."

"Ah ha."

"But what if he can't help me?"

"Then you're no worse off." Norbert was silent for a moment. "Maybe you should start looking for a new job, Steve. Leave under your own terms. You have a few months before things come to a head."

Then he hung up.

Steve put the phone down and rested his head in his hands. The day had started well, but then - due to circumstances beyond his control and despite everything he'd given to Wyxcomb - he'd become expendable.

Five very long minutes passed. It felt like more.

Eventually he looked up, forced a smile onto his face, and dialled Craig Lally.

It was 3:56 p.m.

## Ten Minutes

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve calls Craig, who gives him ten minutes on the phone. Craig can't meet today but arranges to meet tomorrow morning at nine, with Cheryl, in the cafeteria. He tells Steve to bring the FPP requirements binder.*

Craig answered, then paused. "Norbert said you'd call."

Steve thanked him for their earlier chat, then asked if Craig could spare him thirty minutes.

"Thirty minutes? I don't have thirty minutes to spare."

"Fifteen, then?"

"I'm afraid I am very busy just now."

Steve steeled his jaw. "I'm desperate. I swear I wouldn't ask otherwise."

Another pause. "Yes, okay. I'll give you ten minutes on the phone, but no more."

Steve thanked him. He had no intention of sticking to ten minutes.

"We're experiencing a few little problems."

"Little problems? You said you're desperate."

"No, desperate is accurate."

He spent the first eight of his ten minutes explaining why he was so desperate, glossing over the details of the mutiny. Craig summed it up matter-of-factly: Hal expected FPP by December 1st, Steve couldn't predict how long the remaining features would take to build and test, therefore he couldn't keep his promise. Was that it?

"Yes."

"That diagram we drew - Darth. Has anything changed?"

"It's the same, just much worse. The date's shrunk and the only way we can possibly deliver on time is to cut more features and risk a shoddier product."

"Let's use that pressure to our advantage. A noose around one's neck, they say, is very good at focusing one's thoughts."

Steve didn't laugh. It was *his* neck Craig was talking about.

"I can't meet you today," Craig said. "But I want to help. Can you meet me tomorrow morning at nine in the cafeteria? I'll bring Cheryl - you owe her an apology."

Steve winced. He did. 

“Okay.”

"One more thing. Does your team keep a physical copy of the FPP requirements, or is it all on screens these days?"

"There's a binder. A big one. Phil and Vrinda insist on it - they say you can't see the big picture on a screen."

"Bring it with you tomorrow." Craig paused. "I'm on holiday from Friday. Three weeks. So tomorrow morning is all I've got."

"I understand."

"Good. Nine o'clock. And Steve - whatever you do, *don't think about small batches*."

"Small what?"

"Batches. Don't think about them."

Then he hung up.

Huh?

## Clearing the Road

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve doesn't wait for Craig. He picks up the phone and starts clearing the road for FPP - scaling back other projects, pulling in the best people. Gregor already has a list of names.*

Steve looked at the clock. Four Ten PM. Most of his project managers would be packing up to leave.

He couldn't do anything about Craig until tomorrow morning. But there were things he could do right now - things he wished he’d done weeks ago.

Gregor wasn’t his only project manager and FPP wasn't his only project. He had two senior portfolio and programmer managers, five project managers, and between them they managed seventeen other projects, each in various stages of development, each with its own team, its own deadlines, its own stakeholders who believed their project was the most important thing in the world. Under normal circumstances, he'd never dream of raiding them. But Norbert had given him a clear mandate: make FPP work, or start packing.

He picked up the phone and started dialling.

It took him forty minutes and two difficult conversations with the senior managers. He didn't cancel anything outright - he wasn't that reckless - but he put the managers on notice that their projects would likely be cancelled, scaled back or paused within the next two weeks. Their best staff would be redeployed to FPP. He told them that Gregor would be in touch, and that they needed to cascade this to the managers that reported to them.

The conversations were heated, but Steve held firm. When you're running a presidential motorcade, you don't speed the car up. You clear the road. Turn the lights green. Move everything else out of the way. 

FPP was the VIP project, the motorcade, and nothing else mattered. 

He called Gregor.

"I'm going to get you more staff," Steve said. "Developers, testers. As many as you need."

He explained.

Gregor was quiet for a moment. "Good. But I don't just need bodies. I’m gonna need at least two of the other project managers to join my team. More staff means more coordination, and that'll bury me if I try to do it alone."

"Who do you want?"

Gregor had names ready. Of course he did.

"And Vrinda is going to have to pull in more analysts. She’ll know who’s best, but there are some very talented people on the Willow project who'd hit the ground running."

Steve wrote the names down. He'd expected Gregor to be grateful. Instead, Gregor was already three steps ahead, thinking about how to absorb the extra capacity without it turning into chaos. That was why Gregor was good at his job. 

Calm as a cucumber, those notoriously calm vegetables.

He said, “You’re going to get a lot of pushback, Gregor. Blame me.”

He put the phone down and sat back. For the first time since he got the news about Pauline and Chaste, he felt like he was moving forward - not just reacting, but acting.

The people stuff, at least, he knew how to do. Clear the road. Get the best people. Give Gregor what he needs. That was management. 

Craig was a different matter. A quality guy from a different floor with a diagram on a napkin and a story about soup. Steve was betting his career on a hunch from Norbert that this man could see something the rest of them couldn't. 

That was why they invented the fingers crossed emoji.

## The French Fry Revelation

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Cheryl reveals the secret of never running out of chips: cook small batches and replenish on demand. The cafeteria went from near-bankruptcy to a thirty percent increase in customers, a forty percent increase in revenue, and every job saved.*

*Thursday, August 17th. 9 a.m.*

Steve dropped the FPP requirements binder on the cafeteria table with a *thunk*. It was enormous - over three hundred pages in a fat three-ring binder, the kind of document that made people's eyes widen when they first saw it.

Craig was already there, nursing a cup of tea. He watched Steve set the binder down. "You carried that in like a shield."

Steve shrugged. He supposed it was one, in a way. Three hundred pages of signed-off requirements. If things went wrong, he could always point to it and say: *we built what you asked for.*

Craig stood. "Leave it there. Follow me."

They started walking towards the kitchen and he said, "There's just one rule I want you to follow."

"What's that?"

"Do not think about small batches. Okay?"

"You said that yesterday."

"And have you been thinking about them?"

Steve scrunched up his face.  "Not really."

Craig smiled. "Good."

---

Cheryl's eyes narrowed when she saw Steve. "You're back!"

He smiled, apologetically. "I want to hear about the French Fries thing," he said.

She studied his face. Something was different. Last time he'd walked away. This time he looked like he was crawling back.  

"Last time, you heard about our Dog Food Problem," she said. "You remember what that taught us here in the cafeteria?"

"Test your own product. Build quality in as you go, not at the end."

Craig turned to Steve. "You told me your team already does in-process inspections. Tell Cheryl what that means."

"We review the code. Walk through it. Check it looks right."

Cheryl tilted her head. "But do you actually *run* it? Try it out? See if it works the way your customers expect?"

Steve hesitated. "Not at that stage. We test properly later, once there's enough built to test."

She nodded slowly. "We didn't just *look* at the food, Steve. We tasted it. That's what made the difference."

Craig said nothing, but Steve could tell the point had landed exactly where he'd wanted it to.

She clapped her hands together. "Right. That was our first step. But it was only the first step." She led them through to the serving area, past the bain-maries where food sat under hot lamps. "Better food brought us more customers. And more customers brought us a whole new problem."

She pointed at the deep fryers. Three of them, side by side.

"Tell me. What would happen if we ever ran out of chips?" She tapped the nearest fryer. "French fries, Steve. The only thing standing between this cafeteria and a full-blown riot."

"You'd all be strung up outside the castle gates."

She nodded. "Would that happen if you ran out of salad, or soup?"

"Not much. Maybe people would eat chips instead."

"Unspoken rule Number One around here: don't run out of chips. Rule Two: don't serve cold chips." She fixed him with a look. "Some things are sacred, Steve."

Craig had wandered off to inspect a safety notice pinned beside the fryers. He seemed to find it fascinating.

"Our French Fry Revelation happened when Craig asked me why we never ran out of chips and yet we rarely threw chips away from cooking too many." She glanced at Craig, still reading his notice. "You know what I said to him?"

"I've no idea."

"It's obvious! Our serving staff keeps an eye on how many chips are out front, and when it gets too low, they shout and we cook more." She paused. "Craig calls it *replenishing on demand*. The key is that we cooked the chips in small batches."

"Small batches?", said Steve, puzzled, then remembering Craig's cryptic comments earlier.

"Has he asked you not to think about small batches yet?"

Steve looked at Craig, still reading his safety notice. "He has."

"He did the same to me. It was like he was an oyster farmer and he'd planted a bit of grit inside my shell, hoping I'd turn it into a pearl.  Don't think of small batches. Don't think of small batches. " 

She shook her head, remembering. "I spent days trying not to think about small batches. Of course, the opposite happened. I figured out how to cook our vegetables in small batches instead of big ones, like we did with the chips."

Steve nodded, but felt even more confused than when he'd walked in.

Cheryl walked him back past the fryers to the prep area. "We used to cook everything in huge pots. *Big batch* of peas, *big batch* of cabbage, *big batch* of carrots. All went on at half ten. Then we'd move it to the bain-maries and keep it warm until people came for lunch."

"Have you ever tasted two-hour-old cabbage?" she asked.

"My grandmother was an expert at one-hour-old cabbage."

Cheryl laughed - a real laugh, not a polite one - and slapped him on the arm. "Your grandmother would have fitted right in here. Two-hour-old is worse, trust me. The food wasn't too bad if you ate at noon, but by two o'clock it was horrid."

She explained the tricks they'd used to slow the deterioration. Loads more oil in a big pot of curry so it'd sit longer without burning. "Just remember that next time you're tempted by a cheap takeaway," she said.

Steve said he would.

"Nowadays, we prep everything before the rush - clean, peel, slice, dice - and cook the first small batch just before lunch. During service, the servers keep an eye on what's selling. They shout to the kitchen when we need more."

"What happens if you're really busy? Don't you risk not keeping up?"

"Could happen. Doesn't. Can you figure out why?"

Steve put his hand to his chin. "You cook in larger batches when demand is high?"

"Bingo!" she said. "Give the man a cigar."

Strange. That was the second time someone had offered him a cigar that day. Or was it yesterday? The days were blurring together.

There was another problem too, she told him. Some days they'd cook too much soup and not enough lasagna. They'd guess at the start of each week what to produce, and they almost always got it wrong. Craig called it overproduction and underproduction - both bad, but losing sales because you didn't have what customers wanted was usually worse.

"So we started cooking soup in smaller batches," Cheryl said. "If a flavour wasn't popular, we'd put on a different one. If it was selling fast, we'd cook a bigger batch of the same."

She was warming to her story now. She described how her team had conducted what she called "a little clandestine commercial espionage" - taking turns visiting the good lunch shops on the High Street, spying on how they worked. They'd used their early profits to buy two small ovens like the ones Subway used and started baking fresh rolls throughout the lunch break. They'd replaced two bain-maries with a sandwich bar, since they no longer needed the storage space.

Her face beamed. "Not bad, eh?"

Craig said, "Not bad at all."

Steve could see she was proud, and rightly so. By the end of the first month, they were serving thirty percent more customers. Revenue was up forty percent. Every job saved.

It all made perfect sense. To them.

But Steve had no idea how any of it applied to his situation. He didn't know what small batches meant in a software context. And he suspected Craig didn't either. Craig might hope the principles from Cheryl's kitchen applied to software development, but Steve was certainly a guinea pig. For the next few hours, though, he decided he would cross his fingers and hope Craig was right. He had nothing left to lose.

Craig thanked Cheryl and they made their way back to their seats out front. Steve checked his phone. A dozen low-priority emails and a voicemail from Eleanor which more or less told him she'd spoken to Norbert, and that he was to work with Craig or pack his bags. Nice. He powered the phone down in case she called again.

"There's one more thing Cheryl's team did that she forgot to mention," Craig said. "They thinned their menu. Removed all but their most popular dishes. It gave them wiggle room to learn how to work the new way, and they gradually added dishes back over time."

It would be some time before Steve realised how important that step - thinning the menu - would be.

## Requirements Are Really Forecasts

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Craig challenges Steve's assumptions about requirements, scarcity and hoarding. Steve resists, but Craig is right and Steve knows it. Craig leaves - away for three weeks - and gives Steve homework: think about small batches, and call Catherine.*

Craig picked up the FPP requirements binder and weighed it in his hands, flipping through the pages before handing it back.

"Since you're desperate, we'll skip the formal process and figure out whether Cheryl's lessons apply to your work." He tapped the binder. "These things you call *requirements* - I don't think that word means what you think it means."

Steve said nothing.

"If they were genuinely *required*" - Craig made little quote marks with his fingers - "then that implies they were mandatory. And if they were mandatory, how could your customers descope them? How could they possibly be required one minute, then not required the next?"

He let that land.

"When your team wrote this document, it was a forecast. Nothing more than a prediction. Your end date was a forecast too. And forecasts, by definition, will sometimes be right and sometimes be wrong - otherwise we'd call them facts." He leaned forward. "You can blame yourselves when your forecasts are wrong, or you can change the way you work so it matters less."

Steve's face felt hot - from embarrassment or anger, he wasn't sure. As much as his instinct was to fight, Craig was making sense. His voice wanted to rise into a defensive whine, but he fought it. "We put a lot of effort into getting that right."

"But it wasn't right, was it? How many times have you descoped?"

"Twice."

Craig nodded. "How much of that three-hundred-page document will you actually deliver?"

Steve did the maths. "Maybe eighty pages. Maybe a hundred."

Craig's jaw dropped. "I had no idea."

"Few do." Steve allowed himself a small, defiant smile. "But it is the most important third."

"So why did you build the other two thirds?"

"Scarcity. IT resource is scarce. Customers know they'll only get one shot at a project, so they ask us to build every feature they can think of - even ones they'll never use. My eldest daughter does the same with roasted potatoes. She piles them onto her plate, hoarding them, just in case. We've asked her to just take more later if she wants them."

Craig half-laughed, half-snorted. "She now takes them in smaller batches, based on actual demand?"

Steve's jaw fell open. "Oh, yes. Smaller batches."

"Customers hoard because they don't trust they'll get a second chance. They pad the requirements with features they don't really want, to protect the features they do want."

"Packing peanuts," Steve said, surprising himself.

Craig raised an eyebrow.

"Foam stuffing. To protect what's actually valuable in the box."

Craig grinned. "I like that." He stood and checked his watch. "I need to go. I'm on holiday from tomorrow and I have a mountain of things to finish before I leave."

"And then you're not back for three weeks."

"But I have good news. You have all the pieces of the jigsaw in your brain, and I'm certain you can figure this out." He headed for the door, then turned. "Homework: think about how Cheryl's lessons apply to software. Think about small batches. And call Catherine - ask her whether she could have told you what the minimum viable product was right from the start."

Craig's phone buzzed. He glanced at the screen. "Eleanor wants to know how we're getting along. What shall I tell her?"

"Like old friends," Steve said.

Craig repeated his words into the phone, then hung up and shook Steve's hand. He'd be away for three weeks. Steve was on his own.
