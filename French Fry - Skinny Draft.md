## You're Back

Cheryl's eyes narrowed when she saw Steve. "You're back!".

He smiled, apologetically. "I want to hear about the French Fries thing."

She studied his face. Something was different. Last time he'd walked away. This time he looked like he was crawling back. She didn't like that.

"I'm so pleased to see you," she said. "I hope we can help."

She said, "The last time you were here, I told you about our *Dog Food Problem*.  How, tasting and testing our own product as we went, helped us make better food. Higher quality meant happier customers. And less waste. And far higher profits, and also, a little later on, pay rises all round."

Steve nodded.

"Before we get to the 2nd part of our story, the *French Fry Revelation*", Cheryl said, "there's something you said that threw me when we last chatted. Craig and I discussed it  after you left.  We need to ask you to clarify something about how you work."

Craig said, "You told us that your team already does in-process inspections. Explain to us what that means."

"Okay", said Steve. "In simple terms it means that my developers review each others  code.  Some times it's two peers. Sometimes it's a lead developer working through the code with one of their team. It's not just a cursory scan, usually.  It's a very thorough process. They'll often go through several iterations - not unlike an author working with an editor."

"When you say they *Inspect it*?" Cheryl tilted her head. "Does that mean you look at it ... but don't actually taste it?"

 "*Taste it*?" Steve thought a moment. "Are you asking if we *test* the code?"

She nodded slowly. "We didn't just *look* at our food, Steve. We tasted it."

"We do some testing as we go, of course", said Steve. "But you can't properly test until we hit the testing phase, which is where we start to join all of the developers' code together."

Cheryl said, "You find a lot of problems then - in your testing phase - don't you?"

"Yes. That's why we do it."

"Sounds like it should be called your test and rework phase."

Steve said, politely, "Maybe."

## The French Fry Revelation

Cheryl led them through the kitchen to the serving area. She pointed at a row of metal containers sitting in a long, heated unit. "These are bain-maries. They keep the food warm after it's cooked." She pointed at a large roast of beef sitting under hot lamps. "And those lamps keep the roasts warm."

Steve nodded.

"Have you ever tasted two-hour-old cabbage?"

"My grandmother was an expert at one-hour-old cabbage."

Cheryl laughed - a real laugh, not a polite one - and slapped him on the arm. "Your grandmother would have fitted right in here. She probably grew up cooking cabbage for a long time because the old breeds needed to be cooked longer. That's not necessary any more, and tastes have changed. "

She shook her head. "We used to cook everything in huge pots. *Big batch* of peas, *big batch* of cabbage, *big batch* of carrots. All cooked hours before lunch. The food wasn't too bad if you ate at noon, but by two o'clock it was horrid. And if it sat too long it turned into ..."

She waited, eyebrows raised.

"Dog food?"

She slapped him on the arm again. "You've got a great memory, Steve." She explained that they'd also had a forecasting problem. Some days they'd cook too much soup but not enough lasagna. They guessed at the start of each week how much to produce, and they almost always got it wrong.

"So we had two problems. The food went stale because we cooked it too far in advance. And we could never predict what people would actually want to eat." She led them past the bain-maries and over to the deep fryers. Three of them, side by side. "But then we had our French Fry Revelation."

She tapped the nearest fryer. "Tell me. What would happen if we ever ran out of French Fries?"

"You'd all be strung up outside the castle gates."

"Exactamundo. Unspoken rule Number One: don't run out of fries. Rule Two: don't serve cold fries." She fixed him with a look. "Some things are sacred, Steve."

Craig had wandered off to inspect a safety notice pinned beside the fryers. He seemed to find it fascinating, but clearly he was giving Cheryl space to talk.

"Our French Fry Revelation happened when Craig asked me two questions. How come we rarely ran out of fries? And, how come we rarely threw fries away because we'd cooked too many?" She glanced at Craig, still reading his notice. "Any idea what I said to him?"

"I've no idea."

"It's obvious! Our serving staff cooked small batches of fries, and when they started to run low, they cooked some more." She paused. "Obvious, right? *Small batches.*"

"Small batches?" said Steve, repeating her words, repeating Craig's words from earlier.

"Has he asked you not to think about small batches yet?"

Steve looked at Craig, still reading his safety notice. "He has."

"Reverse psychology," she said. "He did the same to me. It was like he was an oyster farmer and he'd planted a bit of grit inside my shell, hoping I'd turn it into a pearl." She shook her head, remembering. "*Don't think of small batches. Don't think of small batches. Don't think of small batches.* At first, I thought there was something wrong with him."

"But then he asked me about the fries, and suddenly I saw it. We cooked fries in small batches. We never ran out because we were always cooking the next batch. And the quality was fantastic because no fry sat around long enough to go soggy. Small batches solved both problems at once."

Steve could see how it worked for fries. But fries were simple. Everything else on the menu was more complicated. "What about the rest of the food?"
## Small Batches

"That took a bit longer to figure out," Cheryl said. "But once Craig had planted that bit of grit, I couldn't stop thinking about it."

She explained what they'd changed. They prepped everything before the lunch rush - cleaned, peeled, washed, sliced, diced. Then they cooked the first small batch just before noon and, during the lunch service, the servers kept an eye on what was selling. When something ran low, they shouted and the cooks put on the next batch.

"What happens if you're really busy?" Steve asked. "Don't you run the risk of the kitchen not keeping up?"

"Can you figure it out?"

He thought for a moment. "You cook bigger batches, based on demand?"

"Give the man a cigar!" She grinned. "And if a soup wasn't popular, we'd swap it for something else. Small batches solved two problems at once: fresher food *and* less waste. We were cooking what people actually wanted to eat, not guessing a week in advance."

She described, with obvious pride, how they'd improved their kitchen. They bought a few inexpensive pot dividers so they could cook three different vegetables in one big pot at the same time. She and her staff took turns visiting the good High Street lunch shops and spied on them to learn how they worked. They used some of their early profits to buy two small ovens - like the ones Subway uses - and started baking small batches of fresh lunch rolls throughout the lunch break. They replaced two of the bain-maries with a sandwich bar, since they no longer needed the storage space.

Her face beamed. "By the end of the first month, we were serving thirty percent more customers. Revenue was up forty percent. Every job saved."

It all made perfect sense. To them.

## Thinning the Menu

But Steve had no idea how any of it applied to his situation. He had no idea what small batches meant in a software context. And he suspected Craig didn't either. Craig might hope the principles from Cheryl's kitchen applied to software development, but Steve didn't like being a guinea pig. He decided to cross his fingers and hope Craig was right. He had no other palatable options.

Steve thanked Cheryl and he and Craig made their way back to their seats out front.

"There's one more thing Cheryl's team did that she forgot to mention," Craig said. "They thinned their menu. Removed all but their most popular dishes. It gave them wiggle room to learn how to work the new way, and they gradually added dishes back over time."

It would be some time before Steve realised how important that step - thinning the menu - would be.
