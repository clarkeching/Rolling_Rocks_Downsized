### Key Structural Decision
Part Four absorbs material from both the original Act 2b (Ch 35-49) AND Act 3 (Ch 50-58). This is the right call — it creates a coherent "transformation" arc that runs from first chunk delivered through to internal launch. Part Five (Resolution) will be shorter and more focused on the ending.

The resequencing works well:
1. Team gets rhythm → 2. Identifies bottleneck → 3. Confidence builds → 4. Eleanor validates → 5. Hal confronts → 6. Financial stakes revealed → 7. Ron validates approach → 8. Catherine masters the new way → 9. Catherine ready to launch → 10. Internal launch

This is better dramatic pacing than the original's chronological order.

### Things to Consider

**1. "Gravity didn't always suck" — the book-title payoff**
In the original (Ch 47), Steve sits on Kelvin's Peak, flicks a pebble over the edge, and thinks: "Interesting. Gravity didn't always suck." This pays off the Ch 4 line "Drip. Drip. Drip. Gravity sucks." and echoes the book title. It's the emotional climax of Act 2b.

The condensed version uses the internal launch ("Walk Before You Run") as the emotional climax instead, which works. But a single line could restore the callback. Something like Steve flicking a pebble or thinking about gravity after learning they'll make December 1st. Worth considering — it's the book-title payoff.

**2. Web app origin**
"Walk Before You Run" has Hal calling the website "pig-ugly" and Steve thinking "Four weeks earlier, they didn't even have a web app." In the original, Ch 53 shows Ron helping Steve plan the web app in 40 minutes using Ron's three-list system — Steve "inverts his first project from scratch." The condensed version implies it happened off-screen. For "the movie version" this is probably fine, but a single line could fill the gap (e.g., "Ron had helped them spin up a web team in forty minutes flat").

**3. Flowmasters**
The concept of self-appointed team coordinators (Ch 45) is cut. Consistent with the project rules — it's a supporting concept, not one of the sacred eight. Fine to leave cut.
