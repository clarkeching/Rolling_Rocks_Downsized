# Part Two Outline — Proposed Rewrite

## The Problem with the Current Draft

The current Part Two (~7,500 words) preserves the **teaching** and cuts the **charm**. That's backwards. The result reads like a competent summary of the plot, but it doesn't feel like a story you'd want to curl up with.

**What's missing:**
- **Chapter 30 is entirely gone.** In the original, after Craig leaves for the wedding rehearsal, Steve drives home. He remembers Fran. He reads Ashley a pink fairy book. He hears Alison's chocolate mousse joke. Mom makes risotto. Then Mom has a serious conversation with him: "You need to start looking at other options." This is the emotional heart of Part Two and it's not in the condensed version at all.
- **The Norbert phone call is too thin.** In the original, we learn that Norbert pulled strings to get Steve transferred to Watt's Bridge after Fran died. He's mentor and protector, not just a boss. The condensed version has the plot beats but not the relationship.
- **The kitchen sink is processing, not living.** The original has Steve alone at the sink for two chapters (31-32), thinking through the problem while doing dishes. The rhythm is meditative — domestic detail, then insight, domestic detail, then insight. The condensed version has the insights but not the rhythm.
- **The team meeting (Ch 34-35) is solid but rushes past Catherine's payoff.** In the original, Catherine formally inspects the first chunk, swaps hats, checks the confirmation letter, says "I am delighted with what I have seen" — and then delivers the warning about pace. The condensed version has the setup and the mic-drop but cuts the middle.

**What's too long:**
- **"Requirements Are Really Forecasts"** at 1,134 words is the longest chapter and still feels Socratic. The teaching is important but can be delivered in fewer words with more friction from Steve.

## Key Principles for Writing

1. **Show scenes, don't summarise them.** If the original has dialogue, keep dialogue. If the original has a physical setting, keep the setting.
2. **Keep character moments even if they don't advance the plot.** Ashley's pink fairy book, Alison's chocolate mousse joke, Mom's risotto — these are why people enjoy the story.
3. **Compress teaching, not character.** Craig's lessons can land in fewer words. Steve's family can't.
4. **Steve resists.** Every time Craig teaches something, Steve pushes back. He's not a passive learner. He fights, sulks, bangs his fist, plays word games. That friction is what makes the teaching feel real.
5. **Fresh prose, same beats.** Don't copy the original's sentences. Write new sentences that hit the same beats. Third person, snappier, but faithful to the story.
6. **Keep the humour.** Fowl/foul. Mother-in-law murder. "Younger men, with stamina." Chocolate mousse. "I don't like it when you say arse." These are the moments people remember.
