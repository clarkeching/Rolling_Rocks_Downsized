## Fowl Mood

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve chickens out of confronting Hal. Instead, he tells Gregor to cancel the compulsory overtime and cancelled vacations. Gregor asks: "What would you have done differently?" Steve has no answer.*

*Late August.*

Steve was in a fowl mood when he made it back to his desk. Yes, *fowl*, not foul. Somewhere between the second and fourth floors, the rational part of his brain clicked in. He turned into a big chicken and pushed the button for the sixth floor instead of the penthouse. Gregor shrugged. He was old enough, ugly enough and grumpy enough to be thick-skinned enough to know that sometimes taking the blame was just part of his job.

Steve shook his head. "But it shouldn't be. We should be better at this."

## Start Looking for a New Job

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve calls Norbert, who delivers brutal news: he can't protect Steve if FPP fails. Norbert's advice: beg Craig Lally for help—"Maybe he can see things the rest of us can't." And start looking for a new job.*

Steve tried to call Eleanor, but she was mid-flight to Sweden with Hal.

So he called Norbert.

Norbert answered immediately and without saying hello: "Can you deliver FPP on December 1st?"

"Realistically?"

"Of course, realistically. We are not playing computer games. You do not have three lives to play with. I ask you again: Will you deliver or not?"

"No."

"That's what I thought." The phone went silent for a moment. "I don't know if I can help you. If FPP doesn't ship, then Hal is gone. It will be difficult for me to protect you under those circumstances."

He may as well have slapped Steve across the face. Norbert wasn't just his boss—he was his mentor and protector. After Fran died, it was Norbert who pulled all the bureaucratic strings and corporate levers necessary to transfer Steve from Glasgow to Watt's Bridge, nearer his home. Eleanor hadn't wanted him—she wanted someone with smaller ambitions—but Norbert had insisted. In Wyxcomb, he said, we looked after our own.

His words hurt. Norbert knew the job market in Watt's Bridge for people like Steve was limited. He knew Steve's family situation. He knew Steve would never relocate his kids and his mother.

Steve let his breath out. "I need your help, Norbert."

He said nothing.

"Did you talk to your Quality guy, Lally."

"Briefly."

Steve chose his words carefully. "He tried to help. Drew some diagrams, took me to the cafeteria to show me something. But I walked out. I wanted practical answers, not napkins."

Norbert said, "He called me after your chat. He said he couldn't help everyone, and that was especially true if they didn't want help. He said that if you ever did decide you wanted help, all you had to do was ask."

"Oh."

"My advice is for you to ask for his help. Beg if you have to, but get his help."

"But what if he can't help me?"

"Then you're no worse off." Norbert was silent for a moment. "Maybe you should start looking for a new job, Steve. Leave under your own terms. You have a few months before things come to a head."

Then he hung up.

"Thirty minutes? I don't have thirty minutes to spare."

"Fifteen minutes, then?"

"I'm afraid I am very busy just now."

Steve steeled his jaw. "I'm desperate. I swear I wouldn't ask otherwise."

## The Dog Food Problem

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Cheryl explains how the cafeteria was about to be outsourced—the food was barely fit for a dog. Craig forced them to eat their own cooking, and within two weeks they'd transformed the quality.*

Craig took Steve back into the cafeteria kitchen to meet Cheryl—the thin, short, middle-aged woman who'd worked there for twenty-eight years.

Cheryl's eyes narrowed when she saw Steve. "You again?"

He'd walked out on her the week before, checking his phone while she was trying to help him. This time, he apologized. Properly.

"I'm sorry how our last meeting ended."

She pursed her lips, studying his face. She must have decided he meant it. "You want to hear about our French Fry Revelation?"

"I do."

She led them through to her little office where the three of them sat.

"First, some context. Three years ago, this cafeteria was about to be outsourced. The food was horrible—barely fit for human consumption. You wouldn't feed it to a dog. Well, not a posh dog; a stray would eat it if there were no alternatives. We called this our Dog Food Problem.

"That's when Craig turned up and forced us to eat our own dog food. Literally. He insisted we eat the same food we served our customers. And once we started tasting our own cooking?" She smiled. "We started caring about it. We tested the food before we served it. Fixed things with a little salt or pepper. Within two weeks, we'd transformed the quality. That won us loads more customers. We made loads more money. We saved our jobs."

Steve nodded. She was clearly proud of the achievement.

"Here's the thing though. Soup is cheap and hard to screw up. But our soup often tasted horrible. You want to know why? No one ever tasted it before serving it. We followed recipes from Head Office to the letter, but we never checked if the food was actually any good."

"Why not?"

"Frankly, we didn't care. None of us ate the food we sold. We worked our hours, what more could you ask from us?"

"What changed?"

"By threatening to outsource our jobs, our bosses gave us a reason to worry. By insisting we eat our own food, Craig gave us a reason to care."

Cheryl reached across and slapped Steve on the arm. "And that didn't just make us better at our jobs. It made us *proud*." It hurt a little, but Steve was still pleased with her warmth.

That was the Dog Food Problem—eat what you serve. Taste the software.

"But that was only the first step," Cheryl said. "Better food brought us more customers. And more customers brought us a whole new problem."

## The French Fry Revelation

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Cheryl reveals the secret of never running out of chips: cook small batches and replenish on demand. By the end of the first month, they were serving 30% more customers. Revenue up 40%. Jobs saved.*

Cheryl led them to the deep fryers.

"Tell me. What would happen if we ever ran out of chips?"

"You'd all be strung up outside the castle gates."

She nodded. "Would that happen if we ran out of salad, or soup, or two-week-old cabbage?"

"My grandmother was an expert at one-hour-old cabbage."

Cheryl laughed—a proper laugh, not a polite one. "Two-week-old is much worse, I promise. But no, no one would care. Maybe people would eat chips instead."

"Unspoken rule Number One around here is: Don't run out of chips. Rule Two is: Don't serve cold chips. Understand?"

Steve smiled. "Some things are sacred."

"Our French Fry Revelation happened when Craig asked me why we never ran out of chips and yet, at the same time, we rarely threw out chips because of cooking too many. You know what I said to him?"

"No idea."

"It's obvious, Steve. Our serving staff keeps an eye on how many chips they've got out front, and when it gets too low, they shout out and we cook more. Craig says we *replenish on demand*." She glanced at Craig. "Has he asked you not to think about small batches yet?"

Steve looked at Craig, but he was eagerly reading a safety instruction notice stuck beside the fryers. "He has."

"He did the same to me. It was like I was an oyster and he'd planted a bit of grit inside my shell, hoping I'd turn it into a pearl."

Cheryl continued. "Nowadays, we prep most of the vegetables before the lunch rush. We cook our first small batch just before lunch, and during the service our servers keep an eye on what's selling. They shout out to the cooks when we need to put on the next batch."

"What happens if you're really busy? Don't you run the risk of not keeping up?"

"That could happen, but it doesn't. Can you figure out why?"

Steve put his hand to his chin. "Do you cook in larger batches, based on demand?"

"Bingo!" she said. "Give the man a cigar!"

Strange. That was the second time someone had offered him a cigar that day.

"The first day was nuts," Cheryl said. "The second day was a little less nuts. And things just kept getting less nuts every day."

By the end of the first month, they were serving thirty percent more customers, revenue was up by forty percent, and since most of their costs were fixed, they were suddenly turning a healthy profit.

They got to keep their jobs.

It all made perfect sense. To them. But Steve had no idea how it applied to his situation. He didn't know what small batches meant in a software context. And he suspected neither did Craig. He may hope the principles Cheryl's team used also applied to software development, but Steve was certainly a guinea pig.

For the next few hours, he decided he would cross his fingers and hope Craig was right. He had nothing left to lose.

Craig thanked Cheryl and they made their way back to their seats out front.

"There's one more thing Cheryl's team did that she forgot to mention," Craig said as they sat down. "They thinned their menu. Removed all but the most popular dishes. It gave them the wiggle room to learn the new way of working, then they gradually added dishes back."

It would be some time before Steve realized how important that step—thinning the menu—would be.

## Requirements Are Really Forecasts

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Craig challenges Steve's assumptions: "requirements" are really just forecasts—predictions that may be wrong. They discuss why customers hoard features like packing peanuts. Then Craig sketches a 2x2 grid showing the top-right corner everyone thinks is impossible.*

Craig picked up the 312-page FPP requirements folder and weighed it in his hands, flipping through the pages before handing it back.

"These things you call *requirements*—I don't think that word means what you think it means."

"Pardon?"

"If they were genuinely *required*"—he made little quote marks with his fingers—"then that implies they were mandatory. And if they were mandatory, then how could your customers descope them? How could they possibly be required one minute, then not required the next?"

Steve's face felt hot—from embarrassment or anger, he wasn't sure. As much as his instinct was to fight, Craig was making sense.

"Here's how I see it, Steve. When your team wrote this document, it was in fact a forecast, nothing more than a prediction or an educated guess."

"We put a lot of effort into getting that right. We did market research, surveys, studied goat entrails and tea leaves and consulted an oracle..."

Craig smiled. "But it wasn't right, was it? How many times have you descoped requirements?"

"Twice."

"So your end date was a forecast too?"

Steve's voice wanted to rise into a defensive whine, but he fought it. "An estimate."

"Same thing." Craig jabbed at the folder with his forefinger. "Forecasts, by definition, will sometimes be right and will sometimes be wrong; otherwise we'd call them facts. Here's the lesson: You can blame yourselves when your forecasted requirements are wrong, or you can change the way you work so it matters less."

Steve exhaled, then banged his fist in the palm of his hand. "You're playing word games with me."

"Am I? How much of that three-hundred-page document will you actually deliver?"

Steve did the maths. "Maybe eighty pages. Maybe a hundred."

Craig's jaw dropped. "I had no idea."

"Few do." Steve allowed himself a small, defiant smile. "But it is the most important third."

Craig frowned. "And by cutting those features, won't that make it harder to sell FPP? Won't you lose customers?"

"Hardly. We descoped the less important features. They look good on a product specification sheet, but very few customers were ever likely to want them. Catherine said early on that we'd definitely never offer the option to pay by check."

"But why would she require that feature in the first place if it's not valuable?"

"Scarcity," Steve said. "IT resource is scarce. I've got a four-year-long backlog of projects waiting to be started. Whenever a new project starts, the customers know they'll only ever get one shot at getting it right. It will be years until they get another wedge of IT resource to enhance it. So what do they do? They ask us to build every feature they can possibly think of—even if they don't think they'll ever use it."

"So FPP's developers built a check payment mechanism on the off-chance that maybe one day they'd need it?"

"Exactly. My eldest daughter does the same with roasted potatoes. She loves them and worries there won't be enough, so she piles them onto her plate, hoarding them, just in case. We've asked her to just take more later if she wants them."

Craig half-laughed, half-snorted. "Ha! She now takes them in smaller batches, based on her actual demand?"

Steve's jaw fell open. "Oh, yes. Smaller batches."

"If it was so easy to stop your daughter's hoarding, why haven't you stopped your customers hoarding?"

"Everyone knows it happens, but customers insist every single feature is top priority and therefore mandatory." He nodded toward the thick requirements folder. "And yet, in a crisis, it took less than four hours to strip those three hundred pages down to one hundred."

Steve sulked for a moment, feeling sorry for himself, then carried on. "Hoarding is one cause of overproduction. Another is that customers know they'll be asked to chop features later, so they add stuff they don't really want in order to protect the features they *do* want."

"Packing peanuts," Craig said. "Foam stuffing to protect what's actually valuable in the box."

Steve nodded slowly. "I suppose they are."

"Now tell me—what's the equivalent of underproduction?"

It only took Steve a moment. "Change requests. Underproduction happens when we don't build something our customer actually wants. Because we didn't forecast it. So they ask us to change it, or add it—and we reject most of those requests because they're too expensive and too late."

Craig pulled out a napkin and sketched that grid again—the one from their first brunch, weeks ago. Quality versus speed. Four boxes. The empty top-right corner.

|                  |    **Slow**    | **Fast**  |
| ---------------- | :------------: | :-------: |
| **High Quality** | ✓ Good product | **?**     |
| **Low Quality**  |                | ✓ On time |

"Remember Darth? You chose the bottom-right corner. On time but shoddy. You told your team quality was negotiable."

Steve nodded. He remembered. He remembered the cloud too—the five boxes, the X at the bottom, the trap he couldn't un-see.

"Quality, done right, doesn't slow you down. It speeds you up. All that rework you do at the end of every project? That's the cost of building quality in *late*. Build it in early, in small batches, and you go faster."

This time, Steve actually listened. He thought of the dart board in the pub. Three darts in the bullseye, each at an unnatural angle—pushed there rather than thrown. That's what the top-right corner felt like. A bullseye you couldn't actually hit. You could cheat your way there, or pretend you'd hit it, but you couldn't really throw the dart and land it cleanly.

Could you?

Craig handed him a homework assignment: Think about how Cheryl's lessons might apply to software. Think about small batches. And call Catherine to ask her a few questions.

Then Craig stood and checked his watch. "I need to leave very soon or my son's wedding rehearsal will be ruined and his new mother-in-law will end up in jail for murdering me." He gathered his things. "My family have flown in from around the world and I'm off the grid for three weeks. But you have all the pieces of the jigsaw in your brain, Steve. I know you'll come up with something."

Out of politeness, Steve added, "Congratulations."

"Thank you. We should talk when I'm back." Craig looked skeptical. "Call me if you need help. And this time, actually call."

Steve checked his phone. He had a voicemail from Eleanor which more or less told him she'd spoken to Norbert, and that he was to work with Craig or pack his bags. Nice.

## Homework at the Kitchen Sink

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve does the dishes and thinks about Cheryl's lessons. His eye wanders around the kitchen and he starts seeing feature bloat everywhere. He realizes the real question isn't what to build—it's how to get customers to stop hoarding and start prioritizing.*

That evening, Steve did the dishes—he and his mum had a deal where she cooked and he cleaned. She was good at cooking and he was not; she didn't like cleaning up and he enjoyed it. Within moments he'd switched into autopilot, freeing his mind to play back the day. The mutiny. The cafeteria. Craig's packing peanuts. The very real possibility he wouldn't have a job in a few months.

Not your typical day at the office.

He'd also been assigned homework—something that hadn't happened in nearly twenty years. Best get on with that.

Like most things in the kitchen, the dish rack was small, and it was soon full. Steve grabbed a fresh dish towel, quickly did the drying, then started washing again. Small batches of dishes, he thought. Then shook his head. Craig had got inside it.

He flipped through the radio until it found a song he recognized, and kept scrubbing.

Craig was right about customers hoarding features like his daughter hoarded roasted potatoes. But knowing *why* they hoarded didn't tell him how to *stop* them hoarding.

He grabbed another pot from the stove and dunked it in the soapy water. His mum had used it for tomorrow's porridge, and even though she'd soaked it, the inside was thick with stuck-on oats. Ick.

While he scrubbed, his eye wandered around the kitchen. His refrigerator kept stuff cold, and he didn't want much more from it than that—other than perhaps making a Star Trek *whisssshhh* sound when the doors opened. His microwave was a tiny thing they used to defrost and reheat, though when they first came out they were sold as super-dooper oven replacements with all sorts of whiz-bang features nobody used. His TV—he couldn't tell you what it said on the spec sheet, but he could tell you the mute button was worn out from overuse while every other button was untouched.

The scrubbing brush he held in his hand did one thing and it did it well.

Everything in his kitchen was either doing one thing brilliantly or had seventeen features nobody asked for. Craig had rewired his eyes. He was seeing it everywhere now.

"There must be a way to get our customers to prioritize," he said aloud to the dishes. "If I can do that, then I can break the vicious circle."

But how? In the cafeteria, they prioritized by cooking more of what customers were actually buying. Steve couldn't just cook up a batch of features cheaply and make more if they sold well. Software didn't work that way.

He scrubbed at the porridge pot, thinking.

## Small Batches Click

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve finds Ashley's half-eaten cereal—she always takes more than she needs. The insight clicks: small batches are simply small projects delivering the most important features first. Disappointingly dull, but right.*

He went to the lounge looking for stray dishes and found two bowls the kids had left. One was empty, the other half-full of soggy cornflakes. That would be his little Ashley—she always took more cereal than she needed, then they ended up wasting it. He'd asked her, many times, to just take a small bowl's worth then get more if she was still hungry.

But she was still young.

He took the bowls back to the kitchen and kept thinking.

He'd told Craig that if only customers prioritized their features at the start of each project, they could build a smallish version of the solution—just big enough to deliver comfortably before the deadline. Then, if they had time and budget left over, they could add the next highest features.

That sounded like cooking up a medium-sized batch followed by smaller ones. Craig had refused to tell him what small batches looked like in software. He said Steve would figure it out.

Could it be something as simple as breaking a medium-sized project down into a bunch of smaller projects?

He thought it could.

Was that all there was to it?

He thought a moment, and decided it was. A small batch was a small project in which they delivered a small selection of features—not just any small selection, but a small selection of the *most important* features.

How disappointingly dull. He'd expected thunderclaps and awe, or maybe a Eureka Moment where he jumped out of his office and ran naked down the street. But there was none of that. Did Cheryl feel similarly underwhelmed when she figured out she shouldn't cook a huge pot of cabbage, but several smaller ones? Probably.

Still, it felt right and it felt good. Steve smiled and said aloud, "Nice."

Small batches were small projects. But that still didn't help him with the prioritization problem.

Or did it?

They couldn't get their customers to prioritize adequately when they did big projects. Would they behave differently if, instead of asking them to lock in twelve months of work, they asked them to set in stone just the first month? What if they told Catherine the analysts, designers, developers and testers were ready to start, and if she didn't tell them what to work on, they'd choose on her behalf and start spending her budget—probably picking the most technically interesting features unless she told them otherwise?

Yes. Small batches would force their customers to prioritize.

In fact, if they could guarantee that each project had a genuine phase two—one that was used to *enhance* rather than just fix bugs—customers would stop hoarding. They'd trust that they could get features later.

But of course, what customer in their right mind would believe that guarantee? They'd been burned too many times.

Steve wiped down the benches and gave the kitchen floor a quick sweep.

Craig was right; small batches were key. But a key is useless without someone to put it in the lock, turn it, and then yank the door open.

## Younger Men, With Stamina

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve calls Catherine and realizes she could have told them the minimum viable product from day one. He thinks about Ron's three-list system, then hunts for a book in his attic—and discovers the inverted pyramid.*

That evening, Steve grabbed his phone from his jacket. Fran used to call it Camilla because, she said, sometimes it was like there were three of us in our relationship. He still smiled every time he picked it up.

He called Catherine—his customer rep, the one with the wild red hair who'd taken Pauline's betrayal so personally. He needed to understand something.

"What else would I, a good-looking single woman in the prime of my life, be doing in the evening?" she said. "Having a social life? Ask away."

"Could you have told us what FPP's minimum viable solution was—the bare bones from the War Room—way back at the start of the project?"

She didn't even pause. "Of course."

"Can you roll forward to December, after FPP is live? Can you tell me what we should be working on next?"

"Sure. My team's put together a spreadsheet of post-implementation work." She rattled off features without pausing for breath: online applications, automated statements, the comparison tool, direct debit setup, the web app they'd descoped. "I can tell you exactly what they are if you give me a minute."

While Catherine pulled up the spreadsheet, Steve wandered into the lounge, phone tucked against his shoulder.

Bit by bit. That's how he built complicated slideshows—revealing concepts piece by piece, taking care not to overwhelm his audience. That's how Ron McKnight's team worked too, shipping small changes one at a time, keeping work-in-progress short. Small batches by another name.

And then, oddly, he thought of Luca—bumping the cab drivers' breakfasts so Phil and Steve could eat first. No big system. Just a man reading the room and responding to whoever needed it most.

Catherine continued talking through her prioritized features, and Steve listened as she described how the system would grow, feature upon feature upon feature.

And then the most important piece of the puzzle revealed itself.

A newspaper sat folded on the couch, front page headline visible above the fold. Something about factory closures. Steve didn't need to read the article to know what it said—that was the clever thing about how reporters wrote. They put the most important stuff at the top, then the next most important, then the next. It gave them and their editors huge flexibility.

He thanked Catherine and hung up. Then he went to his bookcase, looking for a book about marketing he'd read years ago. It had a story in it about the telegraph system.

His mum arrived with a plate of cheese and nuts, in case he was still hungry.

"What are you looking for?"

"A book."

"What book?"

"A book about marketing and communications. It's important."

He checked the first shelf. Not there.

"Some cheese?"

"Maybe in a few minutes." He checked the second shelf.

"Some nuts, then?"

"Maybe later." Third shelf. Plenty of books, but not the one he wanted.

"I may take a lover. Perhaps two," Mum said.

"Uh-hum."

"Younger men, with stamina."

Steve stopped. Gave her his full attention. "What?"

She smiled. She knew she had him. "What's the book called?"

"It's called *Made to Stick*. It's got a story in it about the telegraph system."

"Is it in the attic?"

He dashed up the stairs to the landing, reached up and turned the attic trapdoor latch with his fingertips, and carefully let the door drop open.

Mum followed him up. "Is something wrong?"

"Nothing's wrong. Trust me. Everything is about to fall into place."

She frowned, not understanding.

Steve spoke with his hands emphasizing the important bits. "Look, Ron's team works on lots of little projects. He ships new software several times each month. He's got three lists—bug fixes, small enhancements, medium enhancements—and they deliver a constant stream of completed requests. Every few days, *bam!* Another one shipped. Start the next one. Then a few days later—*bam!*—another one."

He could see she wasn't keeping up, but he kept going anyway. He needed to hear the words spoken aloud to sanity-check them.

"When the FPP thing happened, Ron said he could change his release dates just like that, without any hassle. His team is so much more nimble than my project teams. And why? Because his workload is made up of loads of small requests. His team completes each one, tests the arse off it, makes sure they haven't broken anything, and releases it. Sometimes one at a time, sometimes batched up—it all depends on what makes sense. But they never do big batches. Never."

He stopped. "You see?"

She said, "I don't like it when you say the word arse, Steven."

Well, at least she was listening. He apologized, then stood on tiptoes and reached for the attic ladder.

"Be careful. You'll wake the kids."

He nodded and carefully pulled the ladder down to its full height. It clicked into place.

"But with FPP, it's a big project, right? It's not made up of loads of little requests. At least, that's what we thought. We thought we couldn't do what Ron does. That's what we thought. But we can. At least I think we can."

"I haven't got a clue what you're talking about. Just don't wake the kids. They'll be grumpy in the morning."

Sometimes Steve worried that his mother didn't understand him. Perhaps it was a generational thing. Perhaps it was because he was speaking gibberish.

Steve climbed into the attic, turned on the light, picked his way through old PCs and vacuum-packed baby clothes until he found the boxes of old books. He rooted around until he found what he was looking for.

When he made his way back down the ladder, Mum was still there watching. He flipped through the pages and almost immediately found what he wanted.

That was when Steve had what he would later call his Egyptian Revelation.

He turned the page toward her and smiled. "There it is: the inverted pyramid."

## We Cannot Deliver on December 1st

> [!abstract] Chapter Summary
> *Steve tells the team the truth: December is impossible. He introduces the inverted pyramid—deliver the most important features first, each chunk "good enough to ship." Catherine insists on being involved in prioritization.*

Three weeks earlier, Steve had assembled the leadership team and told them the truth.

Phil leaned in close before the meeting started. "There's a rumour going around that Hal had you fired."

Steve shook his head. "Not yet."

He stood and faced the room. "We cannot deliver FPP on December 1st."

Silence. Tim finally answered. "February was a stretch. December is impossible."

Vrinda said, "We are on a Death March."

Catherine looked at Steve expectantly. "But you're working on a plan, right?"

"I've been working with Craig Lally over the last few weeks, and we've identified a tweak to our existing test processes that will increase our odds of succeeding."

"*Increase* our odds?"

"Honestly, I know this will make things better, but I don't know if we'll make the date."

He walked to the wall and picked six sticky notes—Catherine's highest priority features—and handed them to Gregor. "Hypothetically, how long would it take to deliver a product containing only those six features?"

Gregor looked miserable. He studied them, screwed up his face, then shook his head. "I don't really know. We'd need to plan in more detail. Maybe two weeks if we got lucky. Six or seven if we don't."

Steve took all but one sticky note back. "How long for just that one?"

"Look, Steve, this is unfair. There's too much uncertainty."

"But you'd be able to deliver it by December 1st?"

"Almost certainly."

Steve feigned surprise. "You mean definitely?"

"Well, almost definitely then."

Catherine exploded. "Why the hell did you commit to deliver the entire project on February 1st if you can't even tell me how long it takes to deliver one little feature?" She spun to face Steve. "I thought you guys were supposed to be professionals."

"Professionals!" Gregor blurted. "You guys upstairs wouldn't even know about Chaste's ambush if it weren't for Phil. How do *you* have the nerve to call *yourselves* professionals?"

"Oh right, blame us for this mess. You made a promise which we took in good faith. You're screwing around with my career here—not to mention Wyx-Fin's reputation and profitability."

Steve intervened. "I made that promise, not Gregor. And I admit I've had my fingers crossed since, hoping I'd make good on it. We haven't. I'm sorry."

"Well, I'm sorry too, Steven, but if you can't even commit to delivering that one feature, then I've got to recommend we scrap the entire frigging project." She sat back, folded her arms, and muttered something Steve's mum wouldn't have approved of.

To Steve's surprise, Vrinda jumped to Gregor's defense. "He didn't say he couldn't deliver that feature. He said he couldn't promise with certainty. Earthquakes, fires, floods—they're very unlikely to happen."

Phil stuck his hand up. Really, he was Steve's plant—they'd discussed this before the meeting. Phil was asking if it was a good time to throw in his pre-arranged comment.

"Realistically, it's more likely the messaging technology will blow up in our face."

The whole room turned to stare at him.

"Hey, it's in your risk register. It looked okay during proof-of-concept, but it's never been properly tested. We're guinea pigs."

Catherine folded her arms. "So we won't know if it's an issue until we start testing?"

Tim stepped in. "The project is loaded with question marks. Things we can't know until we test. The messaging system is top of our list."

Catherine unfolded her arms. "That's why we test, right?"

"Of course."

"Then you need to start testing it today."

Steve nodded in what he hoped was a thoughtful manner. "You're right. We must start testing as soon as possible. But we need to start testing more than just the messaging system. And we can't wait until the testing phase starts."

Phil stood, collected the six sticky notes, and stuck them back on the wall. He stared for a minute, then picked up one.

"We have to test this eventually. And let's be honest, rework it—there's no way it works right the first time. Problem is, the testing phase doesn't start for weeks." He took a deep breath. "The good news is we *can* pull some testing forward and start now. If it uncovers bad news, then good! We've found it early, when there's still time to fix it."

Gregor joined Phil at the wall. He peeled off another sticky. "We should do the same with this one. Nasty mainframe changes that haven't been tested."

Soon everyone was at the wall, and after fifteen minutes they'd agreed to spend the next week or so testing and reworking three of the riskier high-value features.

The team rejoined at the table.

"We'll learn a lot about the quality of our earlier work," Catherine said.

Gregor added, "And in a week or so, we'll have a far better idea whether we can hit December 1st."

Steve nodded. "Agreed. We start the testing phase today, with those features as our first small batch. But there's more to it than that.

"Way back in the nineteenth century, journalists invented a new way of writing articles. It's called the inverted pyramid. It let reporters keep their commitments under conditions of high uncertainty.

"Imagine you're a reporter in the Wild West and you've got a hot news story to send to your editor in New York. Before the telegraph, you'd write your story and send it by stagecoach—introduction, middle, conclusion. *It was a sunny morning as the president ate breakfast. He went about his day, ending up at a theater where he was shot. He was rushed to a doctor, and then he died.*

"But then the telegraph arrived. Reporters could transmit in hours rather than weeks. The problem was, the telegraph was unreliable. A twenty-word telegram made it through fine, but three hundred-word articles often got cut off mid-transmission."

He paused and chuckled, genius-like. No one laughed back.

"If the conclusion—that the president died—is missing, the story loses its point," Catherine said. "The story is only useful if transmitted in its entirety."

Steve nodded. "Can you see the connection to how FPP is structured?"

"Our product can't ship—which is when it becomes valuable—until all the testing is done. Is that what you mean?"

"Exactly. The reporters solved their problem by inverting their articles. Flipping them so the most newsworthy paragraph was at the top, then the next most important, and so on. Even if transmission was interrupted halfway through, the editor still had a useful article."

Gregor tilted his head. "Very clever."

Steve let that sink in. "I want us to invert FPP. Deliver the most important paragraph first, then the next most important, and so on until we hit December 1st. Each paragraph—each small batch—must be good enough to ship."

"*Good enough to ship?*"

"Good enough to ship. We won't ship until December 1st, but I want each small chunk fully tested, fully reworked, good enough quality that we *could* ship it. Each chunk complete in itself."

Catherine didn't look happy. "So you're reneging on your War Room promise. Is this just another way of descoping?"

"Yes. It's a severe descoping *now* rather than later. By the time we leave this room, I hope to have pared the project back to just those three stickies. They'll be our first chunk of good-enough-to-ship software. We test and rework until it's ready, then start the next chunk."

"But you can't tell me exactly what I'll get on December 1st?"

"Nope. But if we don't change, it's probable you'll get nothing."

Her eyebrows furrowed. "Are you asking me for permission, Steve, or telling me this is how it's going to be?"

Steve made eye contact with everyone around the table. "I'm saying this is how it has to be."

"In that case," Catherine said, "we'd better decide what goes in this chunk, and then the following ones."

Gregor said, "Phil, Tim and I can do that."

Catherine shook her head. "No. I need to be involved."

"Why?"

"I'm not getting every feature on the wall, right?"

Steve said, "It seems unlikely."

"In that case, I must prioritize to ensure we get the best possible product within the time available."

Gregor said, "You've already prioritized. I thought we'd tackle them in order."

She shook her head and went to the wall. She grabbed one of the top three high-risk features—the one needing mainframe work—and stuck it on the far right. "This is high risk, but lower value than other features. I'd rather increase my odds of having the others and find a manual workaround for this one."

She stepped back, then started rearranging stickies. She crumpled one up and replaced it with three from different parts of the wall.

"We've still got budget until April 1st, right?"

"At least."

"So we can keep working this way after we go live in December?"

Steve hadn't thought that far ahead. "It's your budget."

"In that case"—she moved another bunch of stickies to the right—"I don't need these until shortly after we go live."

The re-planning took another thirty minutes, and Steve sat back and watched as the team restructured the wall. His only contribution was to ask *That would be good enough to ship, right?* every so often. They were sailing into uncharted territory, making this up as they went along, but for the first time in months, the room had energy instead of dread.

In the end, they settled on a tiny chunk—the tip of the iceberg—that would take between one and two weeks to finalize, depending on what ugly issues they uncovered.
