## The Big Picture

This manuscript has a strong emotional spine, a likeable protagonist, and a set of ideas that land through story rather than lecture. The core architecture works. What follows is a chapter-by-chapter set of notes designed to help you expand selectively (you have ~5,000 words of headroom), deepen the sections that feel rushed, and remove the elements that signal “abridgement” rather than “standalone book.”

Three overarching priorities:

1. **Let the middle breathe.** Parts One and Five are paced well. Parts Two, Three, and Four compress too aggressively in places, making Steve’s journey from confusion to competence feel too smooth. The reader needs to feel the struggle, not just the solution.
2. **Earn the insights.** Several lightbulb moments arrive a beat too quickly. When Steve figures something out at the kitchen sink or on a hilltop, the reader should feel like they could almost have got there themselves - but Steve got there first. Right now, a few of those moments feel handed to him rather than earned.
3. **Remove the safety net.** The “Lightbulb Moments” appendix at the end undermines the storytelling. Move it to a separate companion document (see final section of this memo for details).

The Egyptian Revelation - Steve’s discovery of the inverted pyramid - happens almost entirely offscreen. He calls Catherine, thinks about Ron, thinks about newspapers, runs to the attic, finds a book, and announces “There it is: the inverted pyramid.” But we don’t actually see the moment of connection.

This scene should feel like the book’s equivalent of a movie’s “beautiful mind” moment - not flashy, but the quiet thrill of pieces falling into place. You earned this moment with all the setup. Don’t skip the payoff.

Consider whether you need the flash-forward, or whether you could run these events chronologically: Steve pitches the inverted pyramid → team restructures the wall → three weeks of work → transaction complete. ## The Lightbulb Moments Appendix: Move to Separate Document

**Recommendation: Remove the entire “Steve’s Lightbulb Moments” section from the manuscript and publish it as a separate companion document.**

Here’s why:

**It undermines the storytelling.** For ~15,000 words, you trusted the reader to absorb ideas through narrative. The appendix says, in effect, “in case you weren’t paying attention, here’s what you were supposed to learn.” This retroactively repositions the story as a delivery mechanism for the framework, rather than a story that contains a framework. The whole power of a business fable is that the ideas stick because they’re attached to characters and situations, not because they’re summarised in a grid.

**It changes the book’s genre.** With the appendix, the book is a business fable with a reference guide. Without it, it’s a story. Stories are more memorable, more shareable, and more likely to be recommended. “You should read this book about Steve” is a better recommendation than “You should read this book and then study the appendix.”

**It’s roughly 25% of the current word count.** That’s not a small appendix - it’s a second book glued to the first one. Removing it immediately makes the book feel more like a novel and less like a training manual.

**What to do with it:**

Create a standalone companion document called something like “Steve’s Lightbulb Moments: A Quick Reference Guide to Rolling Rocks Downsized.” This becomes:

- A free downloadable PDF on your website (lead magnet)
- A handout for workshops and talks
- A resource you mention at the end of the book: “For a summary of the key ideas in this story, along with chapter references, visit [URL]”

This way, the people who want the reference get it. The people who just want the story get the story. And you get a lead generation asset that drives traffic to your site.

**In the book itself, replace the appendix with a brief author’s note** - maybe 200-300 words. Something like: “This is a work of fiction, but the ideas in it are real. Incremental delivery has been used successfully on projects of all sizes since the 1970s. If you’d like a summary of the key concepts, visit [URL]. If you’d like to talk about how these ideas apply to your situation, I’m at [contact].”

## Line-Level Notes

A few recurring patterns worth watching:

**Dash overuse.** You know about this (it’s in your preferences), but it’s worth flagging specific instances where a comma, colon, or full stop would serve better:

- “He’s jet-lagged - just back from Singapore - and he’s working through his three-step recovery routine” → The first dash is fine; the second could be a comma.
- “His wife Fran died of a brain aneurysm two-and-a-half years earlier while they were vacationing in Rome” → No dashes needed; this reads cleanly as-is.
- “He’s a widower” → Good. Straight declarative. More of this.

**Chapter summary boxes.** The `> [!abstract]` summary blocks at the start of each chapter are useful for you as an author but should probably be removed from the final version. They spoil the chapter’s surprises and make the book feel like a textbook. If you want chapter summaries, put them in the companion document alongside the lightbulb moments.

**“Pardon?”** Steve says “Pardon?” a lot. Count them and cut at least half. It’s a useful beat for showing surprise or confusion, but it becomes a verbal tic if overused. Alternatives: silence, a look, a physical reaction, or simply not reacting at all (which can be more powerful).

**Named characters who appear once.** Declan, Kryz, Brian, Sharon, and Mahjella all appear in single scenes. In a 73,000-word book, this is fine. In a 20,000-word book, each named character costs more of the reader’s attention. Consider whether Declan’s role (delivering the Chaste/Hal news outside the pub) could be given to Phil or Vrinda instead. Similarly, the Mahjella anecdote in the bottleneck chapter could be trimmed - Cheryl’s plate story makes the same point more memorably.

**Tense shifts.** Most of the book is past tense, but the opening chapter uses present tense (“So let me tell you about Steve Abernethy” … “Then his phone rings”). This creates an intimate, storyteller-at-the-pub feel that works well. But make sure the tense is consistent within scenes. A few spots wobble.

## Summary of Expansion Priorities

If you’re adding up to 5,000 words, here’s where I’d spend them, in priority order:

1. **The Egyptian Revelation scene** (~800-1000 words) - Steve connecting Catherine’s list + Ron’s system + the newspaper headline into the inverted pyramid. This is the book’s centrepiece. Show it happening.
2. **“Requirements Are Really Forecasts” chapter** (~500-800 words) - Slow down the five ideas. Let Steve resist before he accepts. Give the 2x2 grid more room.
3. **Split “The Bottleneck” into two chapters** (~500-600 words net new) - Give the Cheryl teaching and the Phil application their own space.
4. **The mutiny at the pub** (~300-500 words) - More sensory detail, more of the team’s emotional state, more time in the discomfort.
5. **The replanning session with sticky notes** (~300-400 words) - Show Catherine making specific trade-off decisions on the wall.
6. **Eleanor’s introduction in “The Rabbit, the Cow, and the Top Hat”** (~100-200 words) - Physical details, mannerisms, something to make her visual.
7. **The launch event in “Walk Before You Run”** (~200-300 words) - Give the actual go-live moment more ceremony.
8. **Steve’s reaction to learning he didn’t invent incremental delivery** (~100-200 words) - Let the emotion land.

Total: approximately 2,900-4,500 words. Well within your 25,000-word ceiling.

## Final Thought

The thing that makes this book work - the thing that would make me recommend it - is that it’s genuinely moving. Steve’s grief, his relationship with his mum, the bacon as coping mechanism, the fairy-tale gatehouse, Alison’s Scooby-Doo wisdom, and that final line from his mum. Those aren’t decoration. They’re the reason someone finishes the book and remembers it.

The ideas are strong and they’re well-proven. But people don’t recommend business books because the frameworks are good. They recommend them because they cared about the characters. You’ve got that. Now trust it, expand the parts that feel rushed, remove the safety net of the appendix, and let the story do the work.
