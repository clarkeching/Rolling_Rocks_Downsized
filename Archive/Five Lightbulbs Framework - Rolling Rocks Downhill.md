# About the Five Lightbulbs Framework

The Five Lightbulbs framework (created by Billy Broas) builds the five key beliefs someone needs before they'll confidently buy. Each "lightbulb" represents a belief that must switch on in the prospect's mind, in sequence.

**💡1 – The Status Quo**

- Belief: "This business understands my problem."
- Claim: "We get you."
- Purpose: Show deep empathy and specificity about their current pain.

**💡2 – The Other Options**

- Belief: "Those options aren't the best for me."
- Claim: "Those options aren't the best for you."
- Purpose: Address the alternatives they're considering and why those fall short.

**💡3 – Our Unique Approach** _(The midpoint revelation)_

- Belief: "This business's approach is best for me."
- Claim: "Our approach is best for you."
- Purpose: Explain your distinctive methodology or insight - not the offer yet, just the _way_ you solve the problem differently.

**💡4 – Our Offer**

- Belief: "I trust this business with my money."
- Claim: "You can trust us with your money."
- Purpose: Describe the actual product/service - what it is, how it works, what's included.

**💡5 – The New Life**

- Belief: "This business knows what I want."
- Claim: "We know what you want."
- Purpose: Paint the vivid before-and-after transformation - tangible and emotional.

# Context: Rolling Rocks Downhill

**The book:** A 73,000-word business novel teaching lean manufacturing, quality management, and Theory of Constraints principles through narrative storytelling.

**The condensed version:** A much shorter 3rd-person retelling (~10,000 words) preserving the educational content and character journey.

**The core story:** Steve Abernethy, a software development manager at Wyxcomb Financials, faces an impossible project deadline. His CEO threatens his job. His team is near mutiny. He learns - from an unlikely mentor (Craig Lally) and a cafeteria manager (Cheryl MacIntyre) - that quality, done right, increases speed. He transforms his project, hits the deadline, and ends up negotiating from strength rather than desperation.

**The midpoint revelation (💡3):** Quality and speed aren't trade-offs. Quality creates speed. Stop building first and inspecting later. Work in small batches. Test early and continuously. Build to demand, not forecast.

**Key characters:**

- **Steve Abernethy** - Software development manager. Hates asking for help. Transforms from terrified fire-fighter to confident leader.
- **Craig Lally** - Quality consultant. Dry, astute. Teaches through questions, not answers.
- **Cheryl MacIntyre** - Cafeteria manager. Warm, practical. Her "French Fry Revelation" provides the key analogy.
- **Hal Gibbet (Halifax)** - CEO. Caustic, demanding. "Not known for taking prisoners."
- **Eleanor Scharlau** - CFO. Steve's direct boss. Manages by exception, doesn't like small talk.
- **Norbert Billings** - Group CIO. Steve's mentor/protector. Forces Steve to seek help.

## The Five Lightbulbs for Rolling Rocks Downhill

### 💡1 – The Status Quo

**Belief:** "This person understands my problem." **Claim:** "We get you."

You're Steve Abernethy. You're standing in your office watching raindrops roll down the window, thinking: _Gravity sucks._

Your biggest project just got an impossible deadline. Your CEO - a man "not known for taking prisoners" - has made it clear: deliver or you're gone. Your boss can't protect you. Your team is on the verge of mutiny. And you're about to do what you always do: declare a "War Room," demand overtime, cancel holidays, and ship something shoddy.

You hate asking for help. Always have. But you're out of ideas.

The testing phase is coming - the phase where schedules go to die. You've budgeted four months; it could stretch to eight. You never know. That's the problem. You _never know_.

Meanwhile, your kids haven't seen you. Your mother is worried. And deep down, it sickens you that you're about to ship a product loaded with defects, just like last time, just like everyone does.

---

### 💡2 – The Other Options

**Belief:** "Those options aren't the best for me." **Claim:** "Those options aren't the best for you."

You've tried everything the textbooks say:

**Cut scope** - You slash features, but no one's properly prioritized, so you cut the wrong things. You still miss the date.

**Work harder** - Your project manager announces compulsory overtime and cancelled holidays. Your best analyst snaps at him: "Time in the office doesn't correlate with productivity." Your team starts throwing darts at the pub instead of working.

**Add more staff** - But adding people to a late project makes it later. You know this.

**Lower quality standards** - "Ship it shoddy, fix it later." Except your CFO's face goes ashen when she realizes what's at stake. This isn't just your job on the line - it's whether the whole company gets sold to the highest bidder.

None of this works. It never has. And now your mentor has hung up the phone after suggesting you start looking for a new job.

---

### 💡3 – Our Unique Approach (The Midpoint Revelation)

**Belief:** "This approach is best for me." **Claim:** "Our approach is best for you."

Then a quality consultant named Craig Lally takes you to the staff cafeteria. And a tiny woman named Cheryl - who manages the kitchen - teaches you something that changes everything.

**Quality, done right, increases speed.**

Not quality _or_ speed. Quality _creates_ speed. The entire quality movement summed up in one sentence.

Cheryl never runs out of chips, and she rarely throws any out. How? Small batches. Replenish on demand. Don't cook a giant pot of cabbage at 9am and hope it's still appetizing at 1pm.

Your software team, you realize, has been cooking giant pots of cabbage. Building everything first, then spending months finding and fixing defects at the end. Men in white coats with rubber mallets, beating dents out of newly-built cars.

The insight hits you while doing dishes: What if you tested as you built? What if you delivered in small batches? What if you found problems when there's still time to fix them cheaply?

It's not a thunderclap. More like switching on the bedroom light at 6am to stop yourself going back to sleep. But it changes everything.

---

### 💡4 – Our Offer

**Belief:** "I trust this with my time/money." **Claim:** "You can trust us."

This is the condensed version of that story. Same lessons Steve learned, fraction of the time.

You'll follow him from terrified fire-fighter to the man who negotiates his own terms with the CEO. You'll learn from Craig's dry questions and Cheryl's practical wisdom. You'll see the team transform from mutiny to celebration dinner and disco.

Every concept is demonstrated, not lectured. The teaching _is_ the pleasure.

It's short. It's a story. And when you finish, you'll know exactly how to apply these ideas to your own impossible deadline.

---

### 💡5 – The New Life

**Belief:** "This person knows what I want." **Claim:** "We know what you want."

Steve's project launches on time. His CEO - the one who threatened his job - now needs him to stay. His Group CIO is telling everyone Steve's a superhero. He gets offered his dream job.

But here's what matters more:

**His team is proud.** Not burned out. Proud. They're heading to a celebration dinner, and then - for the young and young at heart - a disco.

**His delivery dates became predictions, not wishes.** For the first time in his career, a customer requested to work the new way.

**He's in control.** Not reactive. Not fire-fighting. Making decisions from strength, not desperation.

Standing at the launch, Steve thinks: _Professionally speaking, I've never felt more proud or fulfilled in my life._

Later, his mother says: "Fran would be proud of you."

That's what you actually want. Not just to hit the deadline - but to feel like you're in control of your own destiny. To lead a team that's thriving, not surviving. To go home and see your kids.

The impossible deadline? It becomes possible. Not through heroics. Through working smarter.
